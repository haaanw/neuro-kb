# Source Note — DR-007: Intermittent fasting / TRE beyond calorie restriction, and autophagy

- **type:** autonomous web deep-research (agent), primary-source cited

- **report:** [[DR-007]]
- **status:** distilled → owner review

## Verdict

Best-controlled human evidence gives a split answer on whether TRE/IF works "beyond calories." When calories are clamped equal (isocaloric/weight-maintenance/matched-intake designs), TRE produces NO extra weight loss vs normal eating — the 2024 Annals isocaloric RCT (N=41, obesity+dysglycemia) is the cleanest demonstration; most real-world TRE weight loss reflects spontaneous calorie reduction (~7-22%). However, there is credible signal that circadian TIMING (eating early) improves insulin sensitivity, beta-cell function, BP, and oxidative stress even at weight maintenance, anchored by the Sutton/Peterson eTRF crossover (Cell Metab 2018, prediabetic men, eucaloric). This independent-of-calories benefit appears narrow (early window, dysglycemic men) and was NOT reproduced in larger isocaloric trials (Annals 2024; ChronoFast), so it is medium confidence. Early TRE beats late TRE for glucose control (medium-high). Autophagy is where creator framing most outruns evidence: robust induction is rodent/cell-culture; human in-vivo data are sparse, tissue-specific, and partly contradictory (IF raised autophagy markers in mouse liver but not mouse/human muscle), and no specific fasting-hour clinical threshold (e.g., the popular 16-18h claim) is validated in humans. TRE lean-mass loss is real but mitigable with protein+resistance training (meta FFM effect null). Female reproductive-hormone harm concerns lean on young-rodent data; human trials show mostly neutral-to-beneficial androgen effects (helpful in PCOS). ADF is not superior to daily calorie restriction and has worse adherence.
