# Source Note — DR-008: Light dosing for circadian phase shifting vs popular claims

## Verdict

Light resets the SCN via melanopsin ipRGCs (peak ~480 nm). Direction is set by timing relative to core body temperature minimum (CBTmin, ~2-3 h before wake): light after CBTmin (morning) advances phase, before it (evening) delays phase, with an insensitive midday zone. The intensity dose-response is logistic, not linear: Zeitzer 2000 found half-maximal illuminance ~120 lux for both melatonin phase resetting and suppression, with phase-shift saturation ~550 lux and suppression saturation ~200 lux; ~100 lux dim room light already gives ~half the max phase delay and ~88% of max melatonin suppression. So everyday indoor evening light is a meaningful delaying signal, and you don't need 10,000 lux to shift phase. The real basis for 'get morning sunlight' is that outdoor light (cloudy ~1,000-10,000 lux; sun ~50,000-100,000+ lux) far exceeds indoor (~100-500 lux), reliably saturating the morning advancing signal. Intermittent pulses are nearly as effective as continuous light, supporting short doses. Glass and sunglasses reduce dose; blue light is most potent. Where popular framing outruns evidence: precise 5/10/20-30 min cloud-cover numbers are heuristics not trial-validated, and downstream claims (cortisol/metabolism/immunity benefits from the brief morning dose) are mechanism-flavored extrapolations. Clinical light therapy (2,500-10,000 lux, 30 min-2 h, PRC-timed) is a separate, well-characterized therapeutic regime with only conditional/low-quality AASM recommendation strength.
