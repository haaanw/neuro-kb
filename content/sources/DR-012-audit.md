# Source Note — DR-012: CGM 'glucose-spike' narratives in metabolically healthy people

- **type:** autonomous web deep-research (agent), primary-source cited
- **section:** [[Metabolism, Exercise & Nutrition]]
- **report:** [[DR-012]]
- **status:** distilled → owner review

## Verdict

CGMs are validated for diabetes but offer weak, contested value for metabolically healthy people: they are less accurate in the normoglycemic range (~17.6% MARD in one validation), systematically underestimate true glycemic variability, and no strong replicated human evidence shows isolated postprandial spikes in healthy people predict long-term disease. Healthy people already spend ~96% of time in range (70-140 mg/dL) with CV <20%, so most "spikes" creators react to are normal physiology, and harm is more plausibly tied to chronic/repeated dysregulation than single excursions. The promoted habits are better supported than the device but modest and largely acute: post-meal walking has the strongest replicated acute effect (meta-analytic SMD ~0.47-0.55, best within 0-30 min); exercise snacks/breaking up sitting acutely lower postprandial glucose/insulin (mostly shown in obesity/T2D); food-order sequencing (carbs last) lowers postprandial glucose ~29% and insulin iAUC ~44% in a healthy crossover and improves time-in-range; sleep loss rapidly impairs insulin sensitivity (~19-25% after one night). Popular framing outruns evidence when it treats every spike as harmful, claims spikes cause cancer/aging/mood-energy crashes (grey-literature-only), implies CGM numbers predict disease in non-diabetics, and ignores documented CGM-driven anxiety and disordered eating.
