# Source Note — DR-009: GLP-1 agonists for craving and addictive behavior

## Verdict

Convergent preclinical and emerging human evidence supports GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide; tirzepatide less human-tested) reducing craving/consumption across alcohol, nicotine, and food reward. Mechanism is biologically established in animals: GLP-1 receptors sit in mesolimbic reward circuitry (VTA, nucleus accumbens) and brainstem NTS relays, and GLP-1RAs blunt drug/food-evoked dopamine signaling and reduce seeking. Human evidence is real but stage-limited: two small/moderate semaglutide RCTs in alcohol use disorder (Hendershot 2025 n=48 JAMA Psychiatry; Lancet 2026 n=108 in AUD+obesity) show reduced heavy/lab-measured drinking and craving, plus large EHR cohorts (Wang/Volkow) reporting ~50% lower AUD incidence/recurrence and lower opioid overdose risk. Critically, a semaglutide RCT in daily smokers reduced craving but NOT cigarettes/day — a key dissociation showing craving reduction does not equal quitting. Open questions dominate: durability after discontinuation (untested; documented ~60% weight regain within a year makes craving rebound plausible), whether the effect is addiction-specific vs a general appetite/consumption brake, generalizability beyond obese populations, and confounding in observational data. No GLP-1RA is approved for any addiction. Confidence: medium-high for alcohol craving/heavy-drinking in obese populations, medium for the broad cross-addiction claim, low for durability and behavioral (non-ingestive) addictions and human tirzepatide.
