## Verdict

The claim that intermittent moderate physical stressors (cold, HIIT, heat, fasting) "harden" people against psychological stress via hormesis/inoculation is partially supported but weaker than popular framing. The theoretical scaffolding is robust and replicated: cross-stressor adaptation, Dienstbier's physiological toughness, and McEwen's allostatic-load model all describe how INTERMITTENT, RECOVERABLE, CONTROLLABLE arousal can be adaptive while frequent/unrecovered/uncontrollable arousal causes overload. Perceived controllability is the most decisive variable: in animals (Maier/Seligman vmPFC->dorsal raphe circuit) one controllable-stress exposure immunizes against later uncontrollable stress, and in humans subjective control predicts outcomes better than objective control. However, the two cleanest human training RCTs (6-month aerobic n=88; 8-week HIIT n=45) FAILED to confirm transfer to blunted cortisol/autonomic reactivity to lab psychosocial stress. Modality benefits exist but are usually delayed, small, or equivalent to active controls: cold-water immersion reduces self-reported stress only ~12h post (no RCT mood benefit); Wim Hof Method matched (did not beat) a warm-shower/slow-breathing control on depression/anxiety/stress; sauna/whole-body hyperthermia has a real but small-trial antidepressant signal; fasting gives mild mood effects with vulnerability caveats. Resilience gains likely come as much from repeatedly mastering a CONTROLLABLE challenge (self-efficacy/appraisal) as from physiological toughening. The dose-frequency-recovery line between inoculation and allostatic overload is conceptually clear (inverted-U) but quantitatively unestablished in human RCTs.
