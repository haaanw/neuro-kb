# DR-001: SSRIs & the serotonin hypothesis after Moncrieff

## Executive summary

The 2022 Moncrieff et al. umbrella review (Molecular Psychiatry) found no consistent evidence that depression is caused by, or associated with, lowered serotonin concentration or activity. This is a genuine and defensible refutation of the simplistic "chemical-imbalance" / low-serotonin causal model that was marketed to the public. However — and this is the most important framing correction — it does **not** show that SSRIs do not work, nor does it overturn the mechanism of SSRIs. Drug efficacy and disease causation are separate questions; SSRIs can be effective without depression being a serotonin-deficiency disease (just as aspirin helps headaches that are not caused by an aspirin deficiency). The review drew a strong critical response from ~30+ pharmacologists who argued it attacked a strawman (few researchers held the literal low-serotonin model) and that the umbrella-review format aggregated heterogeneous evidence inappropriately.

On efficacy: the best estimate of the placebo-adjusted SSRI/antidepressant effect is **modest but real**. Cipriani 2018 (the largest network meta-analysis, 522 trials, ~116,000 patients) found all 21 antidepressants superior to placebo, with response odds ratios ~1.37–2.13 (overall ~1.6–1.7) and a standardized mean difference (SMD) on the order of ~0.30 — a small effect by Cohen's conventions, corresponding to an NNT of roughly 7–9 for response. Fournier 2010 (patient-level meta-analysis) showed the drug-placebo gap depends strongly on baseline severity: minimal/absent for mild-moderate depression (HDRS <~23–25) and clinically meaningful only at the severe end.

On the delayed-onset / neuroplasticity story: there is solid human and preclinical evidence that SSRIs raise synaptic serotonin within hours but clinical improvement lags 2–4 weeks, which is the central argument for downstream neuroplasticity mechanisms (BDNF, cAMP–CREB signaling, hippocampal neurogenesis, synaptogenesis). The downstream-adaptation framework is well-supported as a phenomenon; the precise causal chain in humans remains partly inferential.

On harms: withdrawal, sexual dysfunction, and emotional blunting are real and common but their magnitudes are contested and frequently overstated or understated depending on the source. Best current placebo-adjusted withdrawal incidence is ~15% (one in six to seven), with severe withdrawal ~1 in 35 (Henssler 2024), versus the higher ~56% figure from Davies & Read 2019 which did not subtract placebo/nocebo and used selected samples. Treatment-emergent sexual dysfunction is genuinely high (~40–65%+ with structured assessment). PSSD (persistent post-SSRI sexual dysfunction) is EMA-recognized (2019) as a real condition but its incidence/prevalence is essentially unquantified. Emotional blunting affects roughly 40–60% of treated patients by survey.

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

### 1. Moncrieff 2022 found no consistent support for the low-serotonin causal model (HIGH)
The umbrella review examined 5-HT/5-HIAA metabolites, plasma tryptophan, 5-HT1A and SERT receptor/transporter studies, tryptophan-depletion experiments, and SERT (5-HTTLPR) gene and gene×environment studies. Across these strands it found no convincing evidence depression is associated with lowered serotonin. Tryptophan depletion did not reliably lower mood in non-vulnerable people; SERT/receptor findings were weak and plausibly confounded by prior antidepressant exposure.
- https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-022-01661-0 (primary; doi:10.1038/s41380-022-01661-0)
- https://kclpure.kcl.ac.uk/portal/en/publications/the-serotonin-theory-of-depression-a-systematic-umbrella-review-o/
- https://www.psypost.org/2022/07/large-systematic-review-finds-no-evidence-for-the-serotonin-hypothesis-of-depression-63565

### 2. Refuting the chemical-imbalance model does NOT refute SSRI efficacy or mechanism (HIGH)
A formal multi-author reply (Jauhar, Cowen, Nutt and ~30 others) argued the review attacked a model few experts still held, presented no new analyses, and that drug efficacy is independent of whether low serotonin causes depression. This is the key "framing outruns evidence" point: popular/creator claims of "SSRIs debunked" conflate causation with treatment efficacy.
- https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-023-02093-0
- https://www.kcl.ac.uk/news/a-response-to-the-serotonin-theory-of-depression-a-systematic-umbrella-review-of-the-evidence
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37322062/

### 3. Cipriani 2018: antidepressants beat placebo, modest effect, NNT ~7–9 (HIGH)
Network meta-analysis of 21 antidepressants, 522 double-blind RCTs, ~116,477 participants. All drugs more efficacious than placebo; response ORs ranged ~1.37–2.13 (overall ~1.6–1.7); overall SMD ~0.30 (small). An SMD ~0.30 / these ORs translate to an NNT in the ~7–9 range for response. Acute treatment only (8 weeks); says nothing about maintenance or function.
- https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(18)31799-9/fulltext
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30264701/

### 4. Effect size depends on baseline severity (Fournier 2010) (HIGH)
Patient-level meta-analysis: drug–placebo difference is <0.20 (Cohen d) for HDRS <~23 and only crosses clinical-significance thresholds at severe baseline (~HDRS 25+). Benefit "may be minimal or nonexistent, on average, in patients with mild or moderate symptoms." Caveat: limited to 6 studies / 718 patients, two drugs (paroxetine, imipramine); the severity-dependence is debated but directionally replicated.
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20051569/
- http://users.stat.umn.edu/~rend0020/Teaching/STAT8801-2010Spring/articles/depression/depression-jama.pdf

### 5. Delayed onset (2–4 weeks) supports a downstream-neuroplasticity mechanism (MEDIUM)
SSRIs raise synaptic serotonin within hours, but symptomatic improvement lags ~2–4 weeks — the central argument that therapeutic action requires slow downstream adaptations: BDNF upregulation, cAMP–CREB signaling, hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptogenesis. In rodents, chronic (not acute) fluoxetine increases dentate-gyrus progenitor proliferation over ~14 days. A 2023 RCT (PET, [11C]UCB-J) found escitalopram increased synaptic density in healthy humans over 3–5 weeks — direct human support for the plasticity timeline. Mechanism as a phenomenon is well-supported; the exact causal chain to mood improvement in humans is still inferential. Note the delayed-onset effect itself has been partly challenged (some benefit emerges in week 1).
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10827655/ (escitalopram synaptic density RCT)
- https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cellular-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fncel.2020.00082/full (BDNF/plasticity review)
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2965105/ (BDNF/CREB latent period, fluoxetine)
- https://www.imprs-tp.mpg.de/107899/Harmer_al17.pdf (Harmer/Cowen cognitive-neuropsychological model)

### 6. Withdrawal: placebo-adjusted incidence ~15% (1 in 6–7); severe ~1 in 35 (HIGH)
Henssler et al. 2024 (Lancet Psychiatry), 79 studies, ~21,000 patients: discontinuation symptoms in ~1 in 3 who stop antidepressants AND ~1 in 6 who stop placebo; subtracting the nonspecific/nocebo effect gives a drug-attributable incidence of ~15% (one in six to seven), with severe symptoms in ~1 in 35. Most cases mild. This is the most rigorous, placebo-controlled estimate.
- https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpsy/article/PIIS2215-0366(24)00133-0/fulltext
- https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1047008
- https://www.healio.com/news/psychiatry/20240613/15-of-patients-experience-antidepressant-discontinuation-symptoms-which-are-mostly-mild

### 7. The higher "56% withdrawal, ~46% severe" figure (Davies & Read 2019) is not placebo-adjusted (MEDIUM)
Davies & Read 2019 reported withdrawal in ~56% and severe in ~46% of those affected, and was influential in driving UK (NICE/RCPsych) guideline changes toward hyperbolic tapering. But it pooled heterogeneous, often selected/online samples without placebo subtraction, so it overstates drug-attributable incidence and especially severity relative to Henssler 2024. Hyperbolic tapering (Horowitz & Taylor) is a reasonable clinical response but the evidence base for specific taper schedules is still thin.
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30292574/
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12315658/ (appraisal/reanalysis)

### 8. Treatment-emergent sexual dysfunction is common (~40–65%+) (HIGH)
Montejo et al. (prospective, n=1022) found ~59% overall incidence with SSRIs/SNRIs (paroxetine highest ~65%); structured questionnaires (PRSexDQ-SALSEX) yield 60–80% among sexually active patients, far above spontaneous reporting (14–40%). Manifestations: reduced libido, delayed/absent orgasm, genital anesthesia. This is during-treatment, usually reversible on discontinuation.
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6007725/
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11293283/

### 9. PSSD is EMA-recognized (2019) but its incidence is essentially unquantified (MEDIUM/LOW)
In 2019 the EMA acknowledged that sexual dysfunction (genital numbness, anorgasmia, low libido) can persist after stopping SSRIs/SNRIs and required label updates. The condition is real and biologically plausible, but prevalence/incidence are unknown; the few estimates (e.g., ~1 in 216, ~4.3/100,000) come from problematic data. Frequently co-occurs with anhedonia/emotional blunting. Reversibility and true frequency are open questions.
- https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11450419/ (barriers to quantifying incidence)
- https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/epidemiology-and-psychiatric-sciences/article/postssri-sexual-dysfunction-other-enduring-sexual-dysfunctions/8343798C6C29E850661020EF0CEA8968
- https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/side-effects/201906/post-ssri-sexual-dysfunction-recognized-as-medical-condition

### 10. Emotional blunting affects ~40–60% of treated patients (MEDIUM)
Goodwin/Price (Oxford) survey: ~46% of treated depressed patients report emotional blunting (slightly higher in men); other estimates 40–60% for SSRIs/SNRIs. Measured via OQuESA. Partly disentangling drug effect from residual depression symptoms is difficult, so causal attribution is imperfect — hence MEDIUM.
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28628765/ (Goodwin et al. survey)
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22387055/ (OQuESA validation)

## Conflicts & uncertainty
- **Moncrieff strawman dispute:** Moncrieff camp says the chemical-imbalance model was genuinely promoted to patients; critics (Jauhar, Cowen, Nutt) say no serious researcher held the literal low-serotonin model and the review adds no new data. Both are partly right: public marketing oversold it; academic psychiatry largely moved on years ago.
- **Withdrawal magnitude:** Davies & Read (~56%) vs Henssler placebo-adjusted (~15%). The gap is driven by placebo/nocebo subtraction and sample selection. Severe-withdrawal rates especially diverge (~46% of affected vs ~1 in 35 overall).
- **Severity-dependence of efficacy:** Fournier-style severity moderation is contested; Cipriani-era analyses find consistent (if small) benefit across severities; the moderation may be partly a placebo-response-rate artifact at lower severity.
- **Delayed onset itself:** challenged by analyses showing measurable benefit in week 1, complicating the strict "2–4 week neuroplasticity gate" narrative.
- **PSSD causality/frequency:** real condition, but spontaneous-report data and no denominator make incidence unknowable at present.

## What not to absorb yet
- "Moncrieff proved SSRIs don't work / are placebos." FALSE conflation — the review is about causation, not efficacy; efficacy evidence (Cipriani) is separate and positive (if modest).
- "Serotonin has nothing to do with depression or SSRIs." Overstatement — SSRIs still act via serotonin transporters; the refuted claim is the *low-serotonin-deficiency causal* model specifically.
- The "56% / ~46% severe" withdrawal figures as the headline incidence — they are not placebo-adjusted; use the ~15% placebo-adjusted figure as the drug-attributable estimate while acknowledging a real severe minority.
- Specific PSSD prevalence numbers — treat all current figures as unreliable/placeholder.
- Strong causal claims that BDNF/neurogenesis *is* the mechanism of antidepressant response in humans — supported as a correlated phenomenon, not proven as the necessary causal pathway.
- Precise hyperbolic-taper schedules as evidence-based — the principle is sound, the dosing specifics are largely expert opinion.
