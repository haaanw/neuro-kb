# DR-007: Intermittent fasting / TRE beyond calorie restriction, and autophagy

## Executive summary

The central, repeatedly tested question is whether time-restricted eating (TRE) and intermittent fasting (IF) deliver metabolic benefits *beyond* the calorie deficit they tend to cause. The best-controlled human evidence gives a split answer:

- **Weight/fat loss:** When calories are clamped equal (isocaloric, weight-maintaining, or matched-intake designs), TRE produces **no extra weight loss** versus a normal eating pattern. Most real-world TRE weight loss is explained by spontaneous calorie reduction (~7–22%) from the shorter eating window (high confidence). The 2024 Annals of Internal Medicine isocaloric RCT (Maruthur/Lin, N=41, obesity + dysglycemia) is the cleanest demonstration: matched calories → no weight or glucose advantage.
- **Glucose/insulin physiology:** There *is* credible signal that **circadian timing** (eating earlier in the day) improves insulin sensitivity, β-cell function, blood pressure, and oxidative stress markers even at weight maintenance. The landmark Sutton/Peterson eTRF crossover (Cell Metabolism 2018, men with prediabetes, eucaloric) is the strongest support. But larger isocaloric trials (Annals 2024; ChronoFast) failed to reproduce broad cardiometabolic benefit, so the "timing-independent-of-calories" claim is **medium**, not high, confidence and appears strongest in dysglycemic men with *early* windows.
- **Early vs late TRE:** Early TRE (eating window shifted toward morning, dinner early) consistently looks better for 24-h glucose and fasting glucose than late TRE. This is one of the more reproducible findings (medium-high), grounded in circadian biology of insulin sensitivity declining across the day.
- **Autophagy:** This is where popular/creator framing most outruns the evidence. The widely repeated "autophagy switches on at 16–18h of fasting in humans" claim is **not established in humans in vivo.** Robust autophagy induction is demonstrated mainly in rodents and in cell culture; human data are sparse, tissue-specific, and partly contradictory (IF activated autophagy markers in mouse *liver* but not in mouse or human *skeletal muscle*). Specific clinical hour-thresholds are essentially extrapolation (low confidence).
- **Risks:** TRE without adequate protein/resistance training can erode fat-free mass (mixed evidence). Concerns about female reproductive hormones are real but largely preclinical/young-rodent-derived; human trials show modest androgen reductions (often beneficial in PCOS) rather than clear harm to estrogen/gonadotropins (medium/low).

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

1. **When calories are held equal, TRE adds no weight-loss benefit; real-world benefit is mostly the calorie deficit.** (Confidence: high)
   - Isocaloric RCT, N=41 obesity + prediabetes/diet-controlled diabetes, 12 wk, 10-h window with 80% calories before 1pm vs ≤16-h usual pattern; matched intake → no difference in weight or glucose homeostasis. Authors: "any effects of TRE on weight in prior studies may be due to reductions in caloric intake."
   - https://www.acpjournals.org/doi/10.7326/M23-3132 ; lay summary https://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/time-restricted-eating-fails-weight-loss-and-glucose-2024a1000fwp

2. **Early TRE (eTRF) can improve insulin sensitivity, β-cell responsiveness, blood pressure, and oxidative stress at weight maintenance — i.e., a timing effect not driven by weight loss.** (Confidence: medium; strong single trial, small N, men only)
   - Sutton, Beyl, Peterson et al., Cell Metabolism 2018. Crossover, men with prediabetes, 6-h eucaloric eating window with dinner before 3pm vs 12-h; food matched meal-by-meal; no weight change. Improved insulin sensitivity and β-cell function, lowered BP, reduced 8-isoprostane.
   - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29754952/ ; https://www.cell.com/cell-metabolism/fulltext/S1550-4131(18)30253-5

3. **Early TRE outperforms late TRE for glucose control.** (Confidence: medium-high)
   - Slovenian 3-arm RCT (2023, Univ. of Primorska): early TRE + energy restriction improved fasting glucose more than late TRE + ER (−0.35 mmol/L, p=0.012) and improved fat mass/BMI/metabolic age.
   - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261561425000950
   - Mechanistic crossover: eating dinner early improved 24-h glucose and next-day lipid metabolism. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8308587/

4. **Reduced calorie intake AND periodic fasting each contribute partly independently to the metabolic effects of caloric restriction (preclinical/mechanistic).** (Confidence: medium for the mechanism, low for direct human translation)
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7189989/

5. **Fasting-induced autophagy is robust in rodents but poorly demonstrated in humans in vivo; effects are tissue-specific.** (Confidence: medium that human evidence is weak/mixed; low for any specific hour-threshold claim)
   - IF activated autophagy markers in mouse liver but NOT in mouse or human skeletal muscle. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722000740
   - 36-h fast reduced (not increased) muscle LC3-I/II and p62 in untrained humans only; human autophagy flux measurable in WBCs only with ex-vivo leupeptin trick. https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/japplphysiol.01146.2017
   - eTRF affected markers of circadian clock, aging, and autophagy (e.g., LC3 mRNA changes) in humans — exploratory marker-level only, not flux. https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/11/6/1234

6. **The popular "autophagy kicks in clinically at ~16–18h of fasting" claim is not validated in humans.** (Confidence: low / creator-overreach flag)
   - No human in-vivo trial establishes a clinically meaningful autophagy threshold by fasting hour; figure derives from rodent and cell-culture kinetics. (Synthesis of the autophagy sources above.)

7. **TRE risk to lean/fat-free mass is real but mitigable; meta-analytic effect on FFM is null on average.** (Confidence: medium)
   - Meta-analysis of RCTs: TRE no significant effect on fat-free mass overall. https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1664412/full
   - TRE + resistance training + high protein preserves FFM while cutting fat. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40796095/ ; https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8157764/

8. **Effects on female reproductive hormones: modest androgen lowering (beneficial in PCOS); no clear harm to estrogen/gonadotropins in human trials. Strong "harms women" framing leans on young-rodent data.** (Confidence: medium for androgen/PCOS findings, low for harm claims)
   - Human review: IF decreased testosterone/free androgen index and raised SHBG in premenopausal women with obesity; no effect on estradiol, gonadotropins, prolactin. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9182756/
   - Pre/postmenopausal TRE sex-hormone study. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9877115/

9. **Alternate-day fasting is not superior to daily calorie restriction for weight loss and has worse adherence (higher dropout).** (Confidence: high)
   - Varady JAMA Intern Med 2017 RCT, N=100, 1 yr: ADF 6.0% loss ≈ daily CR; ADF dropout 38% vs 29% CR. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24215592/ (program); summary https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2017/05/170501112633.htm

## Conflicts & uncertainty

- **Sutton eTRF (2018, positive) vs Annals isocaloric (2024, null):** Both controlled calories. Sutton found timing benefits; Annals did not. Reconciliation: Sutton used a tighter 6-h *very early* window in prediabetic men with a full eucaloric crossover and intensive metabolic phenotyping; Annals used a 10-h window and a parallel design. The timing benefit may be (a) real but small/limited to early extreme windows and dysglycemic phenotypes, or (b) a small-sample artifact. Genuine open question.
- **ChronoFast (isocaloric, women with overweight):** circadian clocks shifted but cardiometabolic health did not improve, reinforcing the null/weak side. https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.10.05.24314120.full.pdf ; https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/scitranslmed.adv6787
- **Autophagy:** species- and tissue-specificity is the core conflict (liver vs muscle; mouse vs human; mRNA marker vs actual flux). No agreed human assay for in-vivo autophagic flux during fasting.
- **Women's risk:** clinical worry vs human-trial reassurance; most alarming data are young-rodent or mechanistic.

## What not to absorb yet

- Any specific number of fasting hours ("16h", "18h", "24h") presented as the point where human autophagy becomes clinically meaningful — not established in humans in vivo.
- The claim that TRE/IF beats calorie restriction for weight loss — controlled trials say it does not, once calories match.
- Blanket "IF is metabolically magic independent of calories" — the strongest independent-of-weight signal is narrow (early window, dysglycemic men) and not reproduced in larger isocaloric trials.
- Strong "IF harms women's hormones/fertility" claims as if human-proven — current human data show mostly neutral-to-beneficial hormone effects; harm signal is largely preclinical.
- Treating creator/blog autophagy/longevity claims (e.g., Inito, lifestyle blogs cited only for context) as primary evidence.
