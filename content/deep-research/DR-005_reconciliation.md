# DR-005 Reconciliation — Deliberate-stress / hormesis resilience transfer

**Question:** Does voluntarily enduring intermittent physical stressors (cold, heat, HIIT, fasting) "train" a more resilient psychological stress response that transfers to ordinary life stress?

**Reports compared:**
- CDR (ChatGPT): `raw_reports/chatgpt/DR-005-CDR.md`
- Agent (autonomous): `raw_reports/agent/DR-005.md`

---

## Agreements (claims both reports make — high confidence)

1. **The strong popular claim is not supported.** Neither report finds that cold, heat, or fasting has healthy-human RCT evidence showing transferable psychological stress resilience to a *different* future stressor. Both call the creator narrative ("physical hardening = mental hardening") overstated.

2. **Exercise has the best transfer case, but the cleanest training RCTs are null.** Both cite the **6-month aerobic RCT (n=88, +9.4% VO₂peak, no between-group effect on cortisol/ACTH/HR/BP reactivity to TSST; both groups habituated)** and an **8-week HIIT-vs-active-stretching RCT (fitness rose, no group difference in stress-test psychoneuroendocrine response; both groups improved mood/self-efficacy non-specifically)**. Both treat these convergent nulls as undercutting the strong claim.

3. **Controllability is the strongest moderator.** Both rank the Maier/Seligman line (vmPFC/prelimbic → dorsal raphe; controllable stress immunizes against later uncontrollable stress) as one of the most robust findings in the area, and both conclude the "voluntariness/control" ingredient may matter more than the physiological toughening itself. (CDR cites Hartley, Limbachia; agent cites Maier & Seligman 2016 reappraisal — same framework.)

4. **Cold-water immersion: delayed, not acute, self-reported stress reduction and no RCT mood benefit.** Both cite the **same CWI systematic review/meta-analysis (11 studies, 3,177 participants)**: stress effect is short-lived (~12 h post-exposure), there is an acute inflammatory rise, only one RCT measured mood (null), and the literature is confounded by combining cold with exercise and by self-selected cold-users.

5. **WHM benefit is confounded by breathing / expectancy / active-control matching.** Both conclude the cold component is not shown to be the active ingredient and that any WHM resilience claim is confounded by breathwork and belief.

6. **Allostatic load / dose-recovery framework is conceptually sound but quantitatively unspecified.** Both cite **McEwen 1998 (PubMed 9629234)** and an inverted-U logic (adaptive when activation is intermittent and recoverable; overload when too-frequent/unrecovered), and both state that **no human RCT has established a precise adaptive dose/recovery threshold** for deliberate stressors. Both warn against "daily cold + HIIT + fasting + sauna" stacking.

7. **Fasting yields at most mild mood effects, not resilience transfer**, with vulnerability caveats and no psychiatric-population trials.

8. **Habituation to a repeated stressor ≠ cross-domain transfer.** Both explicitly separate within-stressor habituation (real) from transfer to a different stressor (weak).

## Unique to CDR (ChatGPT)

- **Klaperski 2014 positive transfer RCT** with full effect sizes (group cortisol F(2,89)=3.58, p=.03, ηp²=.075; within-group d=.64; HR d=.77; HRV d=.54) — the single strongest positive cross-stressor result, absent from the agent report.
- **von Haaren 2015/2016 exam-stress RCTs** (61 sedentary students; reduced LF/HF, higher RMSSD, lower emotional reactivity during a real-life exam) — the only "real-world" (non-lab) transfer evidence cited by either report.
- **WHM endotoxaemia / Kox line and the WHM systematic review** quantifying the field: ~8 trials, 15–48 participants each, 86.4% male, very low quality; cold-alone "insignificant" on epinephrine/cytokines while breathing-with-retention more active.
- **Overtraining-syndrome taxonomy** with explicit recovery timescales: functional overreaching (days–weeks) → non-functional overreaching (weeks–months) → overtraining syndrome (months+); plus Stöggl training-distribution / mitohormesis (mostly low-intensity + limited high-intensity).
- **Acute-harm physiology of CWI** (raises HR, BP, respiration, O₂ uptake, cortisol, norepinephrine) and explicit cardiac-vulnerability caution.
- **Trauma-population caution**: uncontrollable aversive stress can worsen later fear regulation, so "inoculation" language should not be generalised to sensitised fear systems.
- A structured **"verify against primary papers locally"** section (Kox 2014, Buijze cold-shower RCT, Klaperski, von Haaren, Arvidson, Javelle, Schommer/Hellhammer, Dienstbier, Seery).

## Unique to Agent

- **Perceived-control RCT (n=168)**: persistently low *perceived* control → more helplessness/depression/negative affect even absent objective control; ~48% of the uncontrollable group sustained perceived control and were as resilient as the objectively-controlled group. Strongest direct human controllability quantification; absent from CDR.
- **Subjective > objective control** stated as a discrete, high-confidence claim with a dedicated source.
- **Dienstbier physiological-toughness mechanism stated affirmatively** (intermittent stressor training prevents brain catecholamine depletion / helplessness) and rated high-confidence for the theory. (CDR mentions Dienstbier only as an unverified canonical paper to check.)
- **Positive heat/antidepressant trials** (Janssen 2016, Masuda 2005, UCSF 2025) with proposed mechanisms (serotonin, beta-endorphin, BDNF).
- **Cross-sectional inverted-U for cold-water frequency vs mental health** as concrete (if non-causal) dose-shape evidence.
- **WHM differential edge = reduced post-stressor rumination** (needs replication) and the **mechanism-attribution debate** framed explicitly (Dienstbier catecholamine/HPA route vs Maier/Seligman self-efficacy/appraisal route), concluding human data lean toward the controllability/appraisal route.

## Verdict

Convergence is **high on the core conclusions and on the shared evidence base**: both independently land on "modality-dependent, not the clean story creators sell," both anchor the negative case on the *same* two training RCTs (6-month aerobic null, 8-week HIIT null) and the *same* CWI meta-analysis (11 studies / 3,177 participants), and both elevate **controllability** as the decisive moderator and **McEwen allostatic-load dose-recovery** as the right (but unquantified) framework. Those overlapping claims now warrant **higher confidence**: (a) cold/heat/fasting lack healthy-human transfer RCTs; (b) the cleanest exercise/HIIT training RCTs are null for cross-stressor physiological transfer; (c) controllability/appraisal plausibly matters more than physiological "toughening"; (d) no validated human dose/recovery threshold exists; (e) WHM benefit is confounded by breathing/expectancy/active-control matching. The reports **diverge mainly by selection rather than contradiction**: the CDR is the more complete and more skeptical document on exercise (it surfaces the *positive* Klaperski and von Haaren RCTs the agent missed, making the exercise picture genuinely "mixed" rather than "null-leaning") and more conservative on heat/WHM psychology, while the agent adds direct human controllability quantification (n=168 perceived-control RCT) and a small-trial heat antidepressant signal the CDR did not retrieve. Claims warranting **lower / hedged confidence** until reconciled against primaries: the overall sign of exercise transfer (mixed vs null — depends on whether Klaperski/von Haaren survive scrutiny), the heat antidepressant signal (small trials, one preprint/press-release source on the agent side), and the WHM evidence picture (the two reports cite entirely different WHM studies — a 48-male endotoxaemia pilot vs an 84-women psychological RCT — so neither should be treated as "the" WHM evidence without checking both). Net: strong agreement on what *not* to absorb; the agent slightly understates exercise by omitting positive RCTs, and the CDR slightly understates heat by not retrieving the antidepressant trials.
