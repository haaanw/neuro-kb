# DR-012: CGM 'glucose-spike' narratives in metabolically healthy people

## Executive summary

Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) are validated medical devices for diabetes management, but their value for *metabolically healthy* people is much weaker than popular "glucose-spike" content implies. In normoglycemic individuals, CGMs are technically *less* accurate than in the diabetic range (one validation study reported a mean absolute relative difference, MARD, of ~17.6% against venous blood), they systematically *underestimate* true glycemic variability, and there is no strong, replicated human evidence that isolated postprandial spikes in healthy people predict long-term disease. Reference data show healthy people already spend ~96% of time in range (70-140 mg/dL) with a coefficient of variation below ~20%, so most of the "spikes" creators react to are normal physiology.

The behavioral interventions promoted alongside CGMs are better supported than the device's diagnostic value, but the effects are modest, mostly *acute*, and best evidenced in people *with* glucose dysregulation:
- **Post-meal walking** has the strongest, most replicated acute effect: walking soon after a meal lowers the postprandial glucose excursion more than pre-meal exercise (meta-analytic SMD ~0.47-0.55), with the largest benefit when activity starts in the first ~30 minutes.
- **Resistance exercise / "exercise snacks"** (breaking up sitting with brief activity) acutely attenuate postprandial glucose and insulin, mainly demonstrated in adults with obesity or T2D; a single resistance bout does not reliably improve 24-h glucose.
- **Meal/food-order sequencing** (vegetables/protein/fat before carbohydrate, "carbs last") consistently lowers postprandial glucose and insulin excursions in trials (e.g., ~29% lower average glucose, ~44% lower insulin iAUC in one healthy-adult crossover; improved time-in-range in a Diabetes Care study), plausibly via slowed gastric emptying and GLP-1.
- **Sleep loss** reliably and rapidly impairs insulin sensitivity: even a single night of partial sleep deprivation reduces insulin sensitivity (~19-25%), and a week of restriction to 5 h reduces it measurably in healthy men.

Where popular framing outruns evidence: treating every transient spike in a healthy person as harmful; claiming spikes cause cancer, accelerated aging, or mood/energy crashes (these appear in grey/creator literature but not the peer-reviewed base); implying CGM numbers in non-diabetics predict future disease; and the documented downside of CGM-driven anxiety and disordered eating from chasing a "flat" curve.

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

1. **CGMs are less accurate in the normoglycemic range and underestimate glycemic variability.** A 24-h validation of the Enlite sensor in 34 healthy adults found MARD ~17.6% vs venous blood, large between-person variation (median Pearson r = 0.68, range -0.35 to 0.93), and that most variability measures were *lower* on CGM than venous sampling. (high)
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4596806/

2. **Healthy people already sit in a tight glucose range, so most "spikes" are normal physiology.** Reference CGM data in non-diabetics show mean glucose ~98-99 mg/dL, median ~96% time in 70-140 mg/dL, and coefficient of variation <20% considered normal. (high)
   - https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0026049523002445
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12569367/

3. **There is little evidence that isolated postprandial spikes in healthy people predict long-term outcomes; harm is more plausibly linked to chronic/repeated dysregulation.** A 2025 scoping review concludes "significant health outcomes likely stem from long-term frequent spikes rather than isolated acute spikes," and a systematic review/meta-analysis found glycemic variability's associations with cardiometabolic risk in non-diabetics are limited/mixed. (high)
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12569367/
   - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38401227/

4. **Post-meal walking lowers postprandial glucose more than pre-meal exercise; earliest is best.** Meta-analysis (116 participants, with and without T2D): post-meal vs pre-meal SMD 0.47 (95% CI 0.23-0.70); post-meal vs control SMD 0.55 (0.34-0.75); pre-meal vs control non-significant. Optimal window is 0-29 min after the meal. (high)
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10036272/

5. **Brief, frequent activity breaks / "exercise snacks" acutely reduce postprandial glucose and insulin.** Systematic review/meta-analysis in adults with obesity: interrupting sitting every ≤30 min with 2-5 min light-to-moderate walking or simple resistance attenuates postprandial glucose and insulin responses; effect best documented in dysregulated populations. (medium)
   - https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1708301/full
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12677009/

6. **A single resistance bout is not sufficient to durably improve 24-h glucose in some populations.** A trial in prediabetic beta-thalassemia patients found one bout of resistance training did not improve glucose/lipids over the subsequent 24 h, illustrating that acute and chronic effects differ and modality matters. (medium)
   - https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7281490/

7. **Food-order sequencing (carbs last) lowers postprandial glucose and insulin.** A healthy-adult crossover reported ~28.6% lower average post-meal glucose and ~43.8% lower insulin iAUC when vegetables/protein preceded carbs; a Diabetes Care study found carbohydrate-last order improved time-in-range and reduced glycemic variability; proposed mechanism is delayed gastric emptying and enhanced GLP-1. (medium-high)
   - https://www.dovepress.com/postprandial-glucose-and-insulin-response-to-meal-sequence-among-healt-peer-reviewed-fulltext-article-DMSO
   - https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/48/2/e15/157633/Carbohydrates-Last-Food-Order-Improves-Time-in
   - https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7551485/

8. **Sleep loss rapidly impairs insulin sensitivity in healthy adults.** A single night of partial sleep deprivation reduced insulin sensitivity by ~19-25% across metabolic pathways; one week of sleep restriction (~5 h/night) reduced insulin sensitivity in healthy men; 24-h deprivation raised steady-state glucose. (high)
   - https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/95/6/2963/2598810
   - https://diabetesjournals.org/diabetes/article/59/9/2126/14525/Sleep-Restriction-for-1-Week-Reduces-Insulin

9. **CGM use in non-diabetics can drive anxiety and disordered eating, and grey-literature claims (cancer, aging, mood/energy, sleep) outrun the peer-reviewed base.** The scoping review found grey literature added effects (cancer risk, skin aging, hunger/fat storage, mood, memory) absent from medical research, and notes "discrepancies... highlight potential for misinformation"; commentators warn of "glucose-centricity" and CGM reaching "almost cult status." (medium)
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12569367/
   - https://www.acsh.org/news/2025/09/01/glucose-spike-myth-dont-fall-wellness-fad-49694

## Conflicts & uncertainty

- **Device usefulness for healthy people is genuinely contested.** Vendor/wellness sources (Signos, Nutrisense, etc.) frame CGM as broadly insightful; academic and public-health sources (Johns Hopkins, scoping review) say evidence of benefit and predictive validity in non-diabetics is scant. The interventions are plausible to follow *without* a CGM at all.
- **Accuracy vs marketing.** CGMs are FDA-cleared and useful in diabetes, but the ~17.6% MARD and variability *underestimation* in normoglycemia mean small "spikes/dips" a healthy user fixates on may be near or within sensor noise. Newer consumer sensors may differ, and head-to-head non-diabetic accuracy data remain limited.
- **Population transfer.** Much of the strongest interventional data (exercise snacks, some sequencing, resistance) is in T2D/prediabetes/obesity. Effect sizes likely shrink in already-healthy people with tight baseline control.
- **Acute vs chronic.** Most cited effects are single-meal/acute; durable changes in insulin sensitivity, HbA1c, or hard outcomes from these habits in healthy people are less established.
- **Mechanism vs outcome.** Spikes causing endothelial dysfunction/oxidative stress appears in both literatures, but largely from cell-culture/short-term studies (one review noted ~64% of studies used endothelial cells, not human subjects); the jump to clinical outcomes in healthy people is not made by the evidence.

## What not to absorb yet

- That a postprandial glucose "spike" in a metabolically healthy person is inherently harmful or requires correction.
- That CGM readings in non-diabetics predict future diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, dementia, or "accelerated aging."
- That "flattening your glucose curve" is a validated health goal for healthy people, or that small spike differences seen on a consumer CGM are reliably real rather than sensor noise (~17.6% MARD; variability underestimated).
- Creator claims tying spikes to mood/energy crashes, hunger, fat storage, skin aging, or cancer — present in grey literature, absent or unsupported in the peer-reviewed base.
- That a single resistance-exercise session reliably fixes 24-h glucose, or that intervention effect sizes measured in T2D/obesity transfer at the same magnitude to healthy people.
