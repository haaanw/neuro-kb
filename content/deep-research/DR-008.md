# DR-008: Light dosing for circadian phase shifting vs popular claims

## Executive summary

The human circadian pacemaker (suprachiasmatic nucleus, SCN) is reset by light via melanopsin-containing intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs), peak spectral sensitivity ~480 nm. **Timing relative to the core body temperature minimum (CBTmin) determines direction**: light *after* CBTmin (i.e., morning, roughly the late subjective night through wake) produces phase **advances** (sleep earlier); light *before* CBTmin (evening/early night) produces phase **delays** (sleep later); there is a relatively insensitive "dead zone" across the mid-subjective day. CBTmin sits ~2–3 hours before habitual wake.

The **dose-response is logistic (saturating), not linear**, and the system is far more sensitive than popular framing implies. In the landmark Zeitzer et al. (2000) study, the half-maximal illuminance for both phase resetting and melatonin suppression was ~**120 lux**, with phase-shift saturation near ~550 lux and suppression saturation near ~200 lux. Dim room light (~100 lux) produced roughly half the maximal phase delay and ~88% of maximal melatonin suppression. Implication: ordinary indoor evening light meaningfully delays the clock, and you do *not* need 10,000 lux to shift phase.

Intensity matters because indoor (~100–500 lux) and outdoor (cloudy ~1,000–10,000 lux; direct sun ~50,000–100,000+ lux) differ by 1–3 orders of magnitude. This is the kernel of truth in "get morning sunlight": outdoor light is dramatically brighter than indoor light, so even brief outdoor exposure delivers a strong, robust morning advancing signal and a reliable daily zeitgeber. Glass (windows/windshields) and especially sunglasses reduce the dose. Intermittent bright-light pulses are nearly as effective as continuous exposure (~6×15-min pulses ≈ 5 h continuous), which is the strongest mechanistic support for short morning doses.

**Where popular framing outruns evidence**: the precise "10 minutes" (or 5/10/20-30 min by cloud cover) numbers are reasonable rules of thumb extrapolated from chronobiology, not directly validated dosing trials; specific downstream claims ("morning sunlight raises cortisol/improves immunity/metabolism") are weaker, mechanism-flavored extrapolations rather than established outcomes from the morning-light dose itself. Clinical light therapy doses (2,500–10,000 lux, 30 min–2 h) are well established for circadian sleep disorders but represent a different (therapeutic) regime than the everyday entrainment claim.

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

1. **Direction of shift is set by timing vs CBTmin: morning light advances, evening light delays.** (HIGH)
   - Phase advances after CBTmin (max advance ~4 h after CBTmin), delays before it; insensitive zone across subjective day. CBTmin ~2–3 h before wake.
   - https://www.circadiansleepdisorders.org/info/PRC.php
   - https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/phase-response-curve

2. **The dose-response is logistic/saturating with half-max ~120 lux; the system is highly sensitive at ordinary indoor levels.** (HIGH)
   - Zeitzer et al. 2000 (J Physiol): inflection ~120 lux for both resetting and suppression; phase-shift saturates ~550 lux, suppression saturates ~200 lux; ~100 lux gives ~half maximal phase delay and ~88% of max melatonin suppression vs ~9,100 lux. Range tested ~3–9,100 lux, 6.5 h exposure, early biological night.
   - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10922269/
   - https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1469-7793.2000.00695.x

3. **Spectral content matters: short-wavelength (~480 nm, "blue") light is most potent for resetting/suppression via melanopsin.** (HIGH)
   - Melanopsin peak ~480 nm; 460 nm > 555 nm for phase delay, melatonin suppression, alerting at equal photon density (Lockley et al.). Note polychromatic daylight is naturally blue-rich.
   - https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2205301119
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2376768/

4. **Indoor vs outdoor intensities differ by 1–3 orders of magnitude — the real basis for "get outside."** (HIGH)
   - Indoor ~100–500 lux; overcast outdoor ~1,000–2,000+ lux; direct sun ~50,000–100,000+ lux. Outdoor light reliably exceeds saturating levels even on cloudy days.
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8929548/
   - https://www.benkuhn.net/lux/

5. **Intermittent bright-light pulses are nearly as effective as continuous exposure — mechanistic support for short doses.** (HIGH)
   - Gronfier/Rimmer: six 15-min pulses (only ~23% of continuous duration) over ~5 h produced nearly the full resetting of 5 h continuous bright light.
   - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15039146/
   - https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/ajpregu.2000.279.5.R1574

6. **Consensus indoor light recommendations: ≥250 lux melanopic EDI daytime, ≤10 lux evening, ≤1 lux sleep.** (HIGH)
   - 2019 international workshop consensus (Brown et al.), measured at the eye, vertical plane ~1.2 m.
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8929548/

7. **Light through closed eyelids can suppress melatonin and shift phase, but with large attenuation.** (MEDIUM)
   - Through eyelids: melatonin suppressed ~25–56% depending on dose/timing; DLMO shifted ~17–71 min after 60-min exposures. Eyelid transmits a small, blue-shifted fraction; relevant to sleeping-eye light masks and ambient bedroom light.
   - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3469368/
   - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24124400/

8. **Clinical light therapy for circadian sleep disorders uses 2,500–10,000 lux for 30 min–2 h, timed to the PRC.** (MEDIUM-HIGH for efficacy; recommendation strength is weak/low-quality per AASM)
   - Morning bright light advances phase in delayed sleep-wake phase disorder; AASM gives a conditional ("weak for") recommendation, low-quality evidence, usually combined with behavioral measures.
   - https://stanfordhealthcare.org/medical-conditions/sleep/advanced-sleep-phase-syndrome/treatments/bright-light-therapy.html
   - https://www.sleepfoundation.org/light-therapy

9. **The popular "10 minutes of morning sunlight" prescription is a reasonable but loosely specified extrapolation.** (MEDIUM for the core entrainment benefit; LOW for specific minute counts and downstream outcome claims)
   - Huberman-style guidance: ~10 min outdoors within ~1 h of waking (5 min clear / 10 min cloudy / 20–30 min overcast), no sunglasses, not through glass. The "outdoors not through glass, no sunglasses" points are mechanistically sound (intensity + spectrum). The exact minute thresholds and specific cortisol/metabolism/immune outcome claims are extrapolations, not directly validated dosing trials.
   - https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/using-light-for-health

## Conflicts & uncertainty

- **"You need bright light / 10,000 lux" vs sensitivity at ~100 lux.** Clinical/therapeutic protocols use high lux for reliable, fast shifts; but the dose-response work shows ordinary indoor light (~100 lux) already produces substantial effects — especially relevant for *evening* light being a hidden delaying signal. These are not contradictory once you separate "guaranteed strong shift" (high lux) from "the threshold where light starts mattering" (~tens of lux).
- **Through-window claims.** Popular guidance says glass "significantly reduces effectiveness." Glass attenuates and removes UV but transmits much visible/blue light; the bigger loss is sitting indoors away from the window (intensity drops with distance) rather than the glass alone. Magnitude of glass-specific attenuation for circadian dose is not precisely quantified in the sources reviewed (MEDIUM uncertainty).
- **Sex differences / chronotype.** Some evidence women are more sensitive to the brightest light but not dim-to-moderate; individual variability (iris color showed no effect in one delay study) is real but not large enough to overturn the general PRC. (MEDIUM)
- **Cortisol awakening response attribution.** Morning light raising cortisol is plausible but the cortisol awakening response is largely endogenous; attributing it to a 10-min light dose is weakly supported. (LOW)

## What not to absorb yet

- Precise "5/10/20-30 minute" cloud-cover dosing as validated thresholds — treat as heuristics, not trial-derived.
- Specific downstream outcome claims tied to the brief morning dose (improved metabolism, immunity, focus, daytime cortisol "spike") — mechanism-flavored, not established from morning-light dosing RCTs.
- Strong quantitative claims about window-glass attenuation of circadian-effective light — under-specified in primary sources.
- Eyelid/light-mask phase shifting as a robust everyday tool — preliminary, small samples, "preliminary evidence" framing in the source itself.
- That blue-blocking in the evening fully neutralizes screen effects — intensity and timing dominate; spectrum is a modifier, not a switch.
