# DR-009: GLP-1 agonists for craving and addictive behavior

## Executive summary

A convergent body of preclinical and human evidence supports the hypothesis that GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs; semaglutide, and to a lesser human-tested extent tirzepatide) reduce craving and consumption across alcohol, nicotine, and food/behavioral reward. The mechanistic story is biologically plausible and well-replicated in animals: GLP-1 receptors are expressed in mesolimbic reward circuitry (VTA, nucleus accumbens) and in brainstem relays (NTS), and GLP-1RAs attenuate drug- and food-evoked dopamine signaling, reducing motivation/seeking for alcohol, nicotine, sucrose and other rewards across rodent models.

The human evidence is genuinely emerging but more mixed and stage-limited than popular framing suggests. As of mid-2026 the strongest data are: (1) two small-to-moderate RCTs of semaglutide in alcohol use disorder (AUD) — Hendershot 2025 (n=48, JAMA Psychiatry) and a 2026 Lancet trial in AUD + obesity (n=108) — both showing reductions in heavy/lab-measured drinking and craving; and (2) large real-world cohort/target-trial-emulation studies (hundreds of thousands of patients) reporting ~40-56% lower incidence/recurrence of AUD and lower opioid overdose risk. Importantly, a semaglutide RCT in daily smokers found reduced nicotine craving but NO significant reduction in cigarettes/day — a key dissociation showing the craving signal can outrun the consumption signal.

Major open questions: durability after discontinuation (no addiction trial has post-treatment follow-up; weight regain after stopping is well-documented and an analogous craving rebound is plausible but untested), whether effects are addiction-specific vs a general appetite/consummatory "brake," generalizability beyond patients with obesity, and confounding/healthy-adherer bias in observational data. Confidence is highest for alcohol craving/heavy-drinking reduction in obese populations (medium-high), moderate for the broad cross-addiction "anti-consumption" claim, and low for durability and for behavioral (non-substance) addictions.

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

### 1. Mechanism: GLP-1RAs act on mesolimbic reward circuitry to blunt dopamine/reward signaling — ESTABLISHED (preclinical), confidence HIGH (for the mechanism existing; medium for its causal role in human craving)
GLP-1 receptors are present in reward-related dopaminergic areas (VTA, nucleus accumbens) as well as hypothalamic energy-balance nuclei. Peripheral GLP-1 signaling reaches the VTA via the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS). GLP-1RAs reduce food-seeking, suppress reward-circuit dopamine signaling, and reduce motivation for sucrose/chocolate in rodents. Mechanistic reviews tie reduced craving to modulation of dopaminergic reward processing rather than purely peripheral satiety.
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12244148/ (Curbing the appetites... GLP-1 agonists on reward circuitry)
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1043661824002573 (IUPHAR review: GLP-1 and SUDs)
- https://www.jci.org/articles/view/192414 (JCI: GLP-1RAs for AUD)

### 2. Alcohol: semaglutide reduces lab-measured drinking and craving in AUD (Hendershot RCT) — confidence MEDIUM (phase 2, small, low dose)
Phase 2 double-blind RCT, n=48 non-treatment-seeking adults with AUD, 9 weeks, escalating low-dose semaglutide (to 1.0 mg). Primary lab self-administration: reduced grams of alcohol consumed (β=−0.48, p=.01) and peak BAC (β=−0.46, p=.03), medium-to-large effect sizes. Reduced drinks per drinking day (β=−0.41, p=.04) and weekly craving (β=−0.39, p=.01); did NOT significantly reduce average drinks per calendar day. No serious adverse events; AEs largely mild. Authors flag small sample, short duration, low dose.
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11822619/
- https://psychopharmacologyinstitute.com/section/semaglutide-for-alcohol-use-disorder-new-rct-data/

### 3. Alcohol: semaglutide + CBT reduces heavy drinking days in AUD + obesity (2026 Lancet RCT) — confidence MEDIUM
Randomized, placebo-controlled, n=108 treatment-seeking patients with AUD and comorbid obesity, semaglutide weekly for 26 weeks on top of CBT. Heavy drinking days in past 30 fell ~12 days (semaglutide) vs ~8 days (placebo) — about 50% greater reduction; benefit on secondary alcohol-related and somatic outcomes. First RCT-level evidence that a GLP-1RA reduces heavy drinking. First real (NIH/NIAAA-supported) trial in treatment-seekers.
- https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(26)00305-3/fulltext
- https://www.nih.gov/news-events/nih-research-matters/glp-1-plus-therapy-can-reduce-heavy-drinking

### 4. Alcohol: large real-world cohorts show lower AUD incidence/recurrence — confidence MEDIUM (observational, confounding)
Wang/Volkow et al. (Nature Communications 2024), retrospective EHR cohort (~83,825 obese patients; broader analyses >600,000), semaglutide vs other anti-obesity meds associated with ~50% lower 12-month risk of incident and recurrent AUD; an author correction clarified the recurrence finding was the robust one. Other cohort work links GLP-1RAs to lower alcohol-related hospitalizations (~28-36%).
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38806481/
- https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-49655-6 (author correction)

### 5. Nicotine: craving reduced but cigarettes/day NOT reduced (semaglutide RCT) — confidence MEDIUM, and a key dissociation
Phase 2a RCT in non-treatment-seeking daily smokers (≥5 cig/day), 9 weeks low-dose semaglutide. Semaglutide did NOT significantly increase lab smoking resistance or reduce weekly cigarettes/day, but DID reduce nicotine craving and body weight. This is the cleanest evidence that the craving signal can dissociate from actual consumption — a caution against equating "less craving" with "quitting."
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42189538/
A separate target-trial-emulation in type 2 diabetes (Annals of Internal Medicine 2024) found semaglutide associated with lower tobacco-use-disorder-related healthcare encounters/medication use vs comparators (observational).
- https://www.acpjournals.org/doi/10.7326/M23-2718

### 6. Food/behavioral reward: semaglutide reduces food cravings and preference for energy-dense food — confidence MEDIUM-HIGH (food); LOW (other behavioral addictions)
In obesity, semaglutide reduces food cravings and preference for fatty/energy-dense foods; rodent data show reduced motivation for palatable food. Reviews discuss potential in binge eating disorder, but rigorous RCTs in BED and in non-ingestive behavioral addictions (gambling, gaming) are largely absent — extrapolation here is speculative.
- https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772408523029071
- https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/26/22/10974 (GLP-1RAs in binge eating disorder, narrative review)

### 7. Opioids and cannabis: observational signal only — confidence LOW-MEDIUM
Real-world analyses (Qeadan et al., Addiction 2025; Wang/Volkow) report ~40% lower opioid overdose rates and lower cannabis use disorder incidence/relapse among GLP-1RA-prescribed patients. These are retrospective and confounded; no RCTs.
- https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/add.16679
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11412894/ (cannabis use disorder cohort)

### 8. Tirzepatide: strong rodent signal, human data preliminary — confidence LOW (human), MEDIUM (preclinical)
Rodent work shows tirzepatide markedly reduces voluntary alcohol intake, binge drinking and relapse-like behavior, blunting dopamine reward signaling (lateral septum). Human data are limited to retrospective/remote observational analyses (lower self-reported intake, drinks/episode, binge odds) and small ongoing phase 2 trials (e.g., NCT06727331, NCT07292519). No completed RCT for craving/addiction.
- https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.08.26.672374v1.full (rodent, preprint — not peer-reviewed)
- https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-48267-2 (semaglutide & tirzepatide reduce alcohol consumption, observational)

### 9. Durability/safety: weight regain after stopping is documented; addiction durability untested — confidence MEDIUM (weight regain), LOW (craving durability)
Systematic review/meta-regression: ~60% of lost weight regained within 1 year of GLP-1RA cessation (semaglutide highest rebound). No addiction trial has measured craving/drinking after discontinuation; experts explicitly flag this gap. Plausible that craving benefits also fade off-drug, but this is untested. GI adverse effects dominate; addiction-specific safety (e.g., effects in lean/underweight AUD patients, interaction with alcohol-related malnutrition) is under-studied.
- https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370(26)00043-X/fulltext (weight regain meta-regression)
- https://www.sciencemediacentre.org/expert-reaction-to-an-rct-for-semaglutide-in-patients-with-alcohol-use-disorder-and-comorbid-obesity/

## Conflicts & uncertainty

- **Craving vs consumption dissociation.** The smoking RCT (claim 5) reduced craving but not cigarettes/day, while the AUD RCTs reduced both craving and (some) consumption measures. This warns that "GLP-1 kills craving" is not interchangeable with "GLP-1 makes people quit." The effect may be partial and substance-/context-dependent.
- **Addiction-specific vs general consummatory brake.** Experts (Field, Science Media Centre) argue the effect may be a general appetite/consumption "brake" rather than treatment of addiction neurobiology per se; the obesity-restricted populations make this hard to disentangle. Weight loss itself, behavioral support (weekly visits, CBT), and reduced caloric drive could mediate part of the drinking reduction.
- **Observational vs experimental.** The most dramatic numbers (~50% risk reductions) come from EHR cohorts vulnerable to healthy-adherer bias, indication bias, and surveillance differences; the author correction to the Wang/Volkow Nature Comms paper (incidence vs recurrence) illustrates the fragility. RCT effect sizes are real but more modest.
- **Generalizability.** Both AUD RCTs and most cohorts are dominated by patients with obesity/T2D; effects in lean AUD patients, and safety in malnourished heavy drinkers, are essentially unknown.
- **Dose.** The Hendershot and smoking RCTs used low doses (to 1.0 mg); higher doses might produce larger consumption effects but also more AEs — untested for addiction endpoints.
- **Tirzepatide human evidence** is mostly rodent + observational + a non-peer-reviewed preprint; the popular "Mounjaro slashes drinking" framing rests largely on animal data.

## What not to absorb yet

- "GLP-1 agonists cure addiction / are proven addiction treatments." No — no GLP-1RA is FDA-approved for any addiction; all human RCTs are phase 2, small, short, and largely in obese populations.
- "GLP-1s help people quit smoking." Not supported — the dedicated RCT showed NO reduction in cigarettes/day (only craving). Tobacco efficacy is unproven.
- "The ~50% reduction in alcohol use disorder is a drug effect." Treat as association, not established causation — it is from confound-prone EHR cohorts; the RCT effect is real but smaller (heavy-drinking-day reduction ~50% greater than placebo, not a 50% population risk drop).
- "Benefits persist after stopping." Untested for craving/addiction; given documented post-cessation weight regain, durability should be assumed unknown, not durable.
- Tirzepatide and behavioral (non-ingestive) addictions (gambling, gaming, shopping): preclinical/speculative only — do not present as human-validated.
- Specific mechanistic claims like "tirzepatide acts via lateral septum dopamine in humans" — that is rodent/preprint-level.
