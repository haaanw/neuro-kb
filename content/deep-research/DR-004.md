# DR-004: Melatonin: physiologic phase-shifter vs supratherapeutic hypnotic

## Executive summary

Melatonin has two largely distinct pharmacologies that the popular and creator framing routinely conflates. (1) As a **chronobiotic (circadian phase-shifter)**, low, physiologic doses (~0.3–0.5 mg) given at a specific clock time relative to the dim-light melatonin onset (DLMO) reset the central clock via SCN MT1/MT2 receptors. This is the mechanistically robust, replicated use; timing matters far more than dose. (2) As a **hypnotic (sleep-onset aid)**, larger over-the-counter doses (3–10 mg) produce only a small, clinically marginal acceleration of sleep onset (~7 minutes in meta-analysis) and a small increase in total sleep time (~8 minutes) — a real but weak effect that is widely overstated.

By condition, the strongest evidence is for **jet lag** (Cochrane: clearly effective for ≥5 eastward time-zone crossings) and **non-24-hour sleep-wake disorder in blind adults** (the one setting with an FDA-approved agonist, tasimelteon, and positive AASM endorsement). **Delayed sleep-wake phase disorder (DSWPD)** is supported but only by a *weak/conditional* recommendation on *low-quality* evidence in the 2015 AASM guideline. **Shift work disorder** is the weakest of the four — endorsement is older and modest, and real-world efficacy is limited by the difficulty of correctly timing doses.

Two safety/quality problems are real and well documented: (a) **commercial label inaccuracy and contamination** — content ranged from −83% to +478% of label and serotonin was a contaminant in roughly a quarter of products (Erland & Saxena 2017); and (b) **a 530% rise in pediatric ingestions** with hospitalizations and two deaths (CDC MMWR 2022). The **adolescent HPG-axis/puberty concern** is biologically plausible (melatonin interacts with the kisspeptin–GnRH axis) but remains **low-confidence**: long-term human safety studies to date have not shown clear pubertal disruption, and no adequately powered RCT exists. Popular framing that low-dose melatonin is a "reset button," that more is better for sleep, or that it is risk-free for kids all outrun the evidence.

## Key claims & evidence (with confidence + URLs)

**1. Low-dose (0.5 mg) and higher-dose (3.0 mg) melatonin produce similarly sized phase shifts when each is given at its own optimal time; timing relative to DLMO, not dose, governs the effect. (HIGH)**
Burgess et al. (J Physiol/J Pineal Res, 2010) generated full phase-response curves for 0.5 mg vs 3.0 mg over 3 days. Maximum advances occurred when 0.5 mg was taken ~2–4 h before DLMO (≈9–11 h before sleep midpoint); the optimal time is *later* for the lower dose. Both doses yielded similar advances/delays at their respective optimal times. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20410229/

**2. The crossover from phase advance to phase delay on the melatonin PRC sits ~1 h after DLMO (roughly ~1 h before habitual bedtime in entrained people); afternoon/early-evening dosing advances the clock, late-night/early-morning dosing delays it. (HIGH)**
Clinical implications of the melatonin PRC (Burgess & Emens review). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2928905/ and three-pulse 3 mg PRC https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2375577/

**3. As a hypnotic, melatonin's effect is real but small: ~7 min faster sleep onset and ~8 min more total sleep time. (HIGH)**
Ferracioli-Oda et al., meta-analysis of 19 trials / 1683 subjects, PLOS ONE 2013: weighted mean reduction in sleep latency 7.06 min, increase in total sleep time 8.25 min, with improved sleep quality. No clear tolerance over time (unlike benzodiazepines). https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0063773

**4. Jet lag is the best-supported indication; 0.5 mg and 5 mg are similarly effective for adaptation, with doses >5 mg no better; timing (destination bedtime) is critical. (HIGH)**
Cochrane review, Herxheimer & Petrie 2002: melatonin "remarkably effective" for ≥5 time-zone (especially eastward) crossings; 0.5–5 mg similarly effective for jet lag itself, though 5 mg promotes sleep slightly better; mistimed (daytime) dosing can delay adaptation. https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD001520_melatonin-prevention-and-treatment-jet-lag and https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11279722/

**5. AASM guidelines support strategically timed melatonin for DSWPD, N24 in the blind, and (older parameters) jet lag and shift work — but intrinsic-disorder recommendations are WEAK/conditional on LOW-quality evidence. (HIGH for the recommendation status; MEDIUM for underlying efficacy)**
2015 AASM Clinical Practice Guideline for intrinsic CRSWDs (Auger et al., JCSM): GRADE-based; strategically timed melatonin is recommended (weak/"may use") for DSWPD and for N24 in blind adults; evidence quality is low. The guideline explicitly recommends *against* melatonin and other sleep-promoting medications for irregular sleep-wake rhythm disorder in elderly people with dementia. https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/10.5664/jcsm.5100 (summary: https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2015/10/151014161441.htm). Earlier 2007 practice parameters cover jet lag (Standard) and shift work (Guideline): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18041479/

**6. Non-24 in blind adults is the one indication with a regulator-approved circadian agonist (tasimelteon, MT1/MT2), reflecting the strongest mechanistic + clinical case among CRSWDs. (HIGH)**
AASM positive endorsement of timed melatonin for blind N24 adults; tasimelteon approved for the same condition. https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/10.5664/jcsm.5100

**7. OTC melatonin products are frequently mislabeled and sometimes contaminated. (HIGH)**
Erland & Saxena, JCSM 2017: across 30–31 supplements, actual melatonin content ranged from −83% to +478% of label; lot-to-lot variability within a product up to 465%; serotonin (a controlled neuro-active contaminant) detected in 8 products (1–75 µg). https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/full/10.5664/jcsm.6462 and editorial "What They Say Is Often Not What You Get" https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/full/10.5664/jcsm.6434

**8. Pediatric melatonin ingestions rose ~530% in a decade, with rising hospitalizations, ICU care, and deaths — a genuine public-health signal driven by gummy formulations and lax regulation. (HIGH)**
CDC MMWR 2022 (Lelak et al.): 260,435 pediatric ingestions reported to poison centers 2012–2021; annual count rose from 8,337 to 52,563; melatonin became the most-ingested substance reported for children in 2020; 5 children required mechanical ventilation and 2 died. https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/71/wr/mm7122a1.htm Follow-up on ED visits 2019–2022: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10932581/

**9. Chronic high-dose melatonin and adolescent HPG/puberty: biologically plausible concern, but human evidence is reassuring-but-thin; not established harm. (LOW for harm; MEDIUM for "not clearly harmful short/medium term")**
Boafo et al. (Nature & Science of Sleep 2019) lay out the mechanistic worry (melatonin's interaction with kisspeptin–GnRH and the normal pubertal decline in nocturnal melatonin) and note the absence of long-term RCTs. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.2147/NSS.S181365 A 2023 systematic review + GRADE (eClinicalMedicine) found mostly mild short-term adverse effects and no strong signal of pubertal disruption, but flagged low-certainty evidence and called for long-term trials. https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370(23)00260-2/fulltext

## Conflicts & uncertainty

- **Dose for sleep vs phase-shifting.** For *phase shifting*, low and high doses are equivalent when optimally timed (Burgess 2010). For *sleep onset/maintenance*, Ferracioli-Oda found higher doses and longer trials gave somewhat larger effects on latency/total sleep time — a real tension. The reconciliation: higher doses add a modest direct soporific effect but offer no chronobiotic advantage, and they raise the risk of next-day grogginess and spillover phase effects from mistiming.
- **Shift work disorder** is the most contested of the four indications: endorsement rests on older parameters and small studies, and the practical problem of correctly timing doses in rotating shifts undercuts real-world efficacy. Treat as MEDIUM-to-LOW.
- **HPG/puberty in adolescents.** The mechanistic concern is genuine but human data are observational/short-to-medium term; long-term safety studies (1–10.8 yr) report no clear deviation in pubertal development, yet none are adequately powered RCTs. Net: do not assert harm; do not assert safety with confidence.
- **Industry vs clinical sources.** The Council for Responsible Nutrition (industry) reframed the JAMA/CDC pediatric data as merely reflecting "popularity of safe products," which conflicts with the clinical reading of rising hospitalizations and deaths. Weight the CDC/JAMA primary data over the trade-association rebuttal.
- **Label accuracy generalizability.** Erland & Saxena tested Canadian-market products; the magnitude in other markets may differ, but the regulatory gap (sold as a supplement, not a drug, in the US) makes the finding broadly relevant.

## What not to absorb yet

- "Low-dose melatonin reliably *resets* your clock overnight / works like a switch." Phase shifts are real but modest (typically <1 h/day) and require correct timing relative to DLMO over several days; a single mistimed dose can shift the clock the *wrong* direction.
- "More melatonin = better sleep." The hypnotic effect is small and plateaus; high OTC doses (5–10 mg) are commonly supraphysiologic and not supported as superior for the core indications.
- "Melatonin is proven safe long-term for kids/teens." Long-term pediatric safety, especially pubertal/HPG outcomes, is under-researched — currently LOW-confidence on harm *and* on safety.
- "AASM strongly recommends melatonin for DSPD." The intrinsic-disorder recommendations are *weak/conditional* on *low-quality* evidence, not strong endorsements.
- Any claim that melatonin helps irregular sleep-wake rhythm in dementia — the guideline recommends *against* sleep-promoting meds there.
- Treating creator "circadian biohacking" protocols (exact mg/timing claims) as validated; PRC timing is individualized to DLMO, which is rarely measured outside research.
