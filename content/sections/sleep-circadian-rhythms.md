# Sleep & Circadian Rhythms

> Canonical synthesis from 27 ingested episodes (multi-creator: Huberman Lab, Rhonda Patrick / FoundMyFitness, featuring Matt Walker, Samer Hattar, Satchin Panda, Marie-Pierre St-Onge, Michael Grandner, Ashley Mason, Glen Jeffery, Ben Bikman). Confidence-graded; creator claims flagged where they outrun evidence.

## Overview

Sleep and circadian rhythm are treated here as two coupled but distinct systems. Sleep is governed by a two-process model: a homeostatic sleep pressure (process S), driven largely by accumulation of adenosine as a byproduct of neuronal energy metabolism while awake, and a circadian process (process C), an endogenous ~24-hour oscillator that sets the timing of when sleep is permitted and when alertness peaks. The two normally align to produce consolidated nighttime sleep; misalignment (shift work, jet lag, late light exposure, late caffeine) is where most modern sleep dysfunction arises. The architecture of a night is itself structured: roughly 90-minute cycles alternating between non-REM (stages N1-N3, with N3 being slow-wave/deep sleep; the older Rechtschaffen & Kales system split this into stages 1-4) and REM (dream) sleep, with deep NREM front-loaded in the first half of the night and REM back-loaded toward morning.

The functional case for sleep is strong and broad. Deep NREM sleep is associated with autonomic down-regulation (acting like natural blood-pressure medication), glucose/insulin regulation, growth-hormone release, glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste, and the consolidation/transfer of new memories. REM sleep is associated with emotional regulation ("overnight therapy"), creative association, peak testosterone release, and aspects of learning. Selectively depriving either stage produces a distinct deficit profile, which is the evolutionary argument that both stages are non-negotiable. Walker's central reframing across these episodes: quality (continuity, depth, architecture) matters as much as quantity — you cannot fully compensate for poor architecture with more hours, nor for short duration with good quality.

The circadian half of the section is dominated by light biology. Specialized melanopsin-expressing retinal ganglion cells (non-image-forming) signal time-of-day to the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Morning bright light (ideally sunlight, viewed through the eyes, no sunglasses) advances and anchors the clock, triggers an early-day cortisol pulse, and sets a ~16-hour timer toward melatonin onset and sleepiness. Evening light — especially bright artificial/blue light — suppresses melatonin and delays the clock. As Hattar discusses, the intrinsic free-running human period is ~24.2 hours (canonically measured by Czeisler et al. 1999, Science, via forced desynchrony), meaning without daily light anchoring people drift ~0.2 h/day later. Panda and others extend the circadian frame to feeding (time-restricted eating), exercise timing, and metabolic health.

A consistent, defensible practical core emerges across creators: regularity of sleep/wake timing, morning sunlight, dimming/avoiding evening light, keeping the sleeping environment cool, limiting caffeine to the first half of the day, and avoiding alcohol near sleep. Around this evidence-graded core sit several creator emphases — red-light therapy, sauna/heat for slow-wave sleep, specific supplement stacks — that range from plausible-emerging to speculative and are flagged below.

A recurring meta-theme is that the creators (Walker explicitly) have walked back early "sleep or else" alarmism. Occasional bad nights are normal; biology works in averages; nighttime awakenings (even 10+/night, ~30 min total awake) are physiologically normal and not pathological unless frequent or sustained (>20-25 min stuck awake). This page preserves that calibration: the science is real but should not be weaponized into anxiety, which itself harms sleep.

## Key findings by theme

### 1. Sleep architecture & the two-process model
- Sleep alternates in ~90-minute NREM-REM cycles; deep NREM (stage N3, formerly stages 3-4) dominates the first half of the night, REM dominates the second half. **[high]**
- Sleepiness is driven by adenosine, a byproduct of neuronal energy use, which both inhibits wake-promoting circuits and activates sleep-promoting circuits via A1/A2 receptors. **[high]**
- Two-process model: homeostatic pressure (process S, adenosine) + circadian timing (process C) jointly determine sleep timing/depth. **[high]**
- If you skip the early (deep-sleep) part of the night and sleep only later, you get mostly REM and remain deep-sleep deficient — sleep does not rigidly "restart from scene one," it tracks both pressure and circadian phase. **[high]** (Walker, gbQFSMayJxk)
- REM is "paradoxical": brain activity resembles waking while the body is paralyzed (alpha motor neuron inhibition), sparing only extraocular and inner-ear muscles. **[high]**
- Sleep efficiency (% of time in bed asleep) above ~85% is the healthy target; brief awakenings totaling ~30 min/night are normal. **[high]**

### 2. Functions of deep NREM vs REM (why both matter)
- Deep NREM supports glucose/insulin regulation, autonomic down-regulation (lower heart rate/BP — "natural blood-pressure medication"), and memory consolidation. **[high]**
- Glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste (including amyloid-beta) is enhanced during deep sleep; chronic sleep loss is linked to dementia risk. **[medium]** (mechanism established in animals; causal human link to dementia is correlational — 76z4UPt-OME)
- REM supports emotional regulation ("overnight therapy"/"emotional first aid"), some learning/memory, and creative recombination. **[medium-high]**
- Peak testosterone release occurs right before and during REM; growth hormone release is substantially REM- and deep-sleep-dependent. **[medium]** (Walker; alcohol can cut overnight GH release >50%)
- Walker notes that sleep disruption accompanies essentially every major psychiatric disorder. **[medium]** (Walker characterization) Sleep and mental health are tightly bidirectionally linked. **[high]**
- Selectively depriving deep sleep vs REM yields distinct deficit profiles, the evolutionary argument that both stages are preserved and necessary. **[high]**

### 3. Circadian timing & light biology
- Melanopsin retinal ganglion cells (non-image-forming) signal time-of-day to the SCN; light must reach the eyes, not skin, to set the central clock. **[high]**
- Morning bright light triggers an early-day cortisol pulse (healthy when early) and sets a ~16-hour timer toward melatonin onset/sleepiness. **[high]**
- The intrinsic free-running human circadian period averages ~24.2 h (canonical measurement: Czeisler et al. 1999, Science, forced desynchrony); without daily light anchoring you drift ~0.2 h/day (~1 h every 5 days) out of sync. **[high]** (as discussed by Hattar, 2XMb3MD6g6Q)
- Outdoor light is far brighter than indoor light even on overcast days (thousands of lux outdoors vs ~500 indoors); perception badly underestimates this. **[high]**
- Evening/late-night bright and blue light suppresses melatonin and delays the clock. **[high]**
- Seasonal melatonin signaling tracks day length across the year, influencing mood and reproductive physiology. **[medium]**

### 4. Caffeine & adenosine
- Caffeine is a competitive adenosine-receptor antagonist — it blocks (doesn't activate) the receptor, masking accumulated sleep pressure without removing the adenosine. **[high]**
- Caffeine half-life ~5-6 h, quarter-life ~10-12 h; clearance speed varies by CYP1A2 liver-enzyme genotype (explains "I can drink espresso at night and sleep fine"). **[high]**
- The "caffeine crash" occurs when caffeine clears and the masked adenosine — plus what accumulated meanwhile — hits at once. **[high]**
- Late caffeine can reduce deep-sleep depth by up to ~30% even in people who fall and stay asleep — subjective "fine" sleep can be objectively degraded. **[medium-high]** (Walker; the ~30% figure is a specific creator claim, directionally supported)
- Practical cutoff: stop caffeine ~8-10 h before bedtime. **[medium]**

### 5. Alcohol & sleep
- Alcohol is a sedative, not a sleep aid: it induces sedation (loss of consciousness), which is not naturalistic sleep. **[high]**
- Alcohol fragments sleep (more awakenings, many unremembered) partly via sympathetic activation. **[high]**
- Alcohol is a potent REM suppressor and can drop overnight growth-hormone release by >50%; even a single evening glass measurably reduces REM. **[medium-high]** (Walker)

### 6. Temperature, heat & sleep
- Core body temperature must drop ~1-3°F to initiate and maintain deep sleep; a cool sleeping environment aids this. **[high]**
- Warming the body before bed (hot bath/sauna, then cooling) paradoxically drops core temp by dilating peripheral vessels, which can speed sleep onset and increase slow-wave sleep. **[medium]**
- Sauna/heat protocols have been reported to acutely raise growth hormone severalfold (dose/temperature-dependent). **[low-medium]** (Patrick, rHKIs5Kcyo0 — acute hormonal spikes; durable sleep/health benefit less established)

### 7. Sleep loss, metabolism & insulin
- Short sleep impairs glucose regulation and insulin sensitivity; exercise partially offsets the impairment. **[high]** (TSIYaargcvE)
- Sleep restriction increases caloric intake (~300 kcal/day more in lab studies) and is associated with modest weight gain over weeks. **[high]** (St-Onge, jjaFnKtytqI)
- Late-night eating plus elevated melatonin may impair the insulin response (melatonin-receptor effect on the pancreas), arguing against large late meals. **[medium]** (2zXks8iePes)
- Deep-sleep deprivation specifically degrades next-day glycemic control toward a pre-diabetic profile. **[medium-high]** (Walker)

### 8. Insomnia, naps & behavioral management
- Clinical rule of thumb: difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep for ~30+ min, occurring frequently, flags possible insomnia disorder. **[high]** (Grandner, AQF_eopP1ys)
- Sleep restriction therapy (compressing time-in-bed toward actual sleep time to rebuild sleep efficiency, then expanding) is a core CBT-I tool. **[high]** (Grandner)
- Polyphasic "Uberman"-style schedules are detrimental across performance, physiological, and sleep-quality metrics — fight biology, you lose. **[high]** (Walker)
- Short naps (~20 min, early-to-mid afternoon) can restore alertness; avoid long/late naps that erode nighttime sleep pressure. **[medium-high]**
- CBT-I and mindfulness-based approaches outperform alcohol/sedatives for chronic insomnia. **[high]** (Mason, f20HNxZdGvU)

## Actionable protocols

Consolidated and deduplicated from the auto-extracted protocol sentences, with timing and caveats.

**Morning light (highest-leverage circadian anchor)**
- View bright light, ideally sunlight, within the first 30-60 min of waking (sooner is better). Clear day: ~5 min; light cloud: ~10 min; heavy overcast/rain: ~20-30 min. **[high]**
- Through the eyes, no sunglasses; do NOT look directly at the sun. Prescription glasses/contacts are fine; avoid viewing through windows/windshields (cuts effective intensity). Indoor light is generally insufficient — get outside.
- Stack with morning exercise facing east for combined wakefulness cues. Doing it daily is best (Hattar).

**Evening light**
- Dim and minimize bright/overhead/blue light in the ~2-3 h before bed to protect melatonin onset; favor low, warm light. **[high]**

**Temperature**
- Keep the bedroom cool to allow the required 1-3°F core-temp drop. **[high]**
- Optional: hot bath (~104°F / 40°C, shoulders-down, 20-30 min) or sauna (~176°F+ / 80°C+, ~20 min) ending 1-2 h before bed, allowing time to cool — promotes slow-wave sleep via post-warming vasodilation. **[medium]** Caveat: heat-driven GH spikes are acute; don't overstate durable benefit. Avoid if it raises core temp too close to bedtime.
- A 1-3 min cold shower in the morning can be used as a wake-up tool (adrenaline release) — not a sleep tool. **[low-medium]**

**Caffeine**
- Confine to the first half of the day; stop ~8-10 h before bedtime (genotype-dependent — adjust to your sensitivity). **[medium-high]** Caveat: "I sleep fine after late coffee" doesn't mean deep-sleep depth is preserved.

**Alcohol**
- Avoid near bedtime; even one evening drink fragments sleep and cuts REM. There is no clearly "safe for sleep" dose. **[high]**

**Naps**
- ~20 min, early-to-mid afternoon, only if it doesn't impair night sleep; skip if you have insomnia (preserve sleep pressure). **[medium]**

**Regularity & nighttime awakenings**
- Keep consistent sleep/wake times (Walker uses an alarm mainly to protect regularity). **[high]**
- Treat brief awakenings as normal; don't catastrophize. Only investigate if you're regularly stuck awake >20-25 min or waking consciously 6-8+ times/night. Avoid phone/bright light during awakenings. **[high]**

**Insomnia**
- Prefer CBT-I (including sleep restriction/stimulus control) and mindfulness over alcohol or OTC sedatives. **[high]**

**Food timing**
- Avoid large late-night meals; late eating plus high melatonin may blunt insulin response. **[medium]**

## Contested / where creator framing outruns evidence

- **Specific quantitative claims** (caffeine reducing deep sleep "by 30%," "aging you 10-12 years"; >50% GH drop from alcohol; specific sauna GH multipliers) are presented confidently by creators. Directionally supported, but exact magnitudes are single-study or context-dependent — treat as illustrative, not precise. **[low-medium]**
- **Red-light therapy** (Jeffery, iT8W6kaD-RA; Huberman essentials): claims of mitochondrial/vision improvement and metabolic effects from short red-light exposure are emerging and intriguing but rest on small studies; durable health benefits and optimal dosing are not established. **[low]**
- **Sauna/heat for slow-wave sleep and growth hormone** (Patrick): acute hormonal spikes are real but their translation into meaningful long-term sleep or health outcomes is extrapolated. **[low-medium]**
- **"Sleep or else" early alarmism**: Walker explicitly retracts his earlier headstrong framing — single bad nights are not catastrophic; biology averages out. Older Walker quotes circulating online overstate per-night stakes. **[high — this is the calibrated view]**
- **Strict caffeine cutoff hours (8-10 h)**: a reasonable default, but genotype variation is large; the precise number is a heuristic, not a universal rule. **[medium]**
- **Late-eating/melatonin-insulin interaction**: mechanistically plausible (pancreatic melatonin receptors), but the practical magnitude for normal eaters is uncertain. **[medium]**
- **Cross-creator supplement stacks** (magnesium, apigenin, theanine, etc.) appear in some episodes as personal protocols rather than evidence-graded recommendations; not synthesized here as established. **[low]**

## Deep Research audit — Melatonin: physiologic phase-shifter vs supratherapeutic hypnotic (DR-004)

> Autonomous web deep-research (agent), cited to primary sources. Full report: [[DR-004]]. Note: [[DR-004-audit]]

**Executive summary:** Melatonin has two distinct pharmacologies that popular framing conflates. As a chronobiotic, low physiologic doses (~0.3-0.5 mg) timed relative to DLMO reset the SCN clock via MT1/MT2 receptors; timing matters far more than dose, and 0.5 mg equals 3.0 mg in phase-shifting power when each is optimally timed (Burgess 2010, HIGH). As a hypnotic, larger OTC doses (3-10 mg) yield only a small effect (~7 min faster sleep onset, ~8 min more sleep; Ferracioli-Oda 2013, HIGH) that is widely overstated. By condition: jet lag is best supported (Cochrane: effective for >=5 eastward zones; 0.5-5 mg similar, >5 mg no better) and non-24 in blind adults is the one indication with an approved agonist (tasimelteon); DSWPD is supported only by a weak AASM recommendation on low-quality evidence; shift work disorder is weakest. Two real safety/quality problems: commercial label inaccuracy (-83% to +478% of label) with serotonin contamination in ~1/4 of products (Erland & Saxena 2017), and a 530% rise in pediatric ingestions with hospitalizations and two deaths (CDC MMWR 2022). The adolescent HPG/puberty concern is biologically plausible (kisspeptin-GnRH interaction) but remains LOW-confidence: long-term human data are reassuring-but-thin with no adequately powered RCT. Creator claims that low-dose melatonin is a reliable "reset button," that more is better for sleep, or that it is risk-free for kids all outrun the evidence.

**Key findings:**
- **[high]** Low-dose (0.5 mg) and higher-dose (3.0 mg) melatonin produce similarly sized circadian phase shifts when each is given at its own optimal time relative to DLMO; timing, not dose, governs the chronobiotic effect. Max advances occur ~2-4 h before DLMO. — <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20410229/>
- **[high]** The melatonin phase-response curve crossover from advance to delay sits ~1 h after DLMO; afternoon/early-evening dosing advances the clock, late-night/early-morning dosing delays it. — <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2928905/>
- **[high]** As a hypnotic, melatonin's effect is real but small: meta-analysis of 19 trials/1683 subjects found ~7 min faster sleep onset and ~8 min more total sleep time, with no clear tolerance. — <https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0063773>
- **[high]** Jet lag is the best-supported indication: melatonin is effective for >=5 time-zone (especially eastward) crossings; 0.5 mg and 5 mg are similarly effective and >5 mg is no better; mistimed daytime dosing can delay adaptation. — <https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD001520_melatonin-prevention-and-treatment-jet-lag>
- **[high]** The 2015 AASM guideline supports strategically timed melatonin for DSWPD and for non-24 in blind adults, but as WEAK/conditional recommendations on LOW-quality evidence; it recommends AGAINST sleep-promoting meds for irregular sleep-wake rhythm in elderly dementia. — <https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/10.5664/jcsm.5100>
- **[high]** OTC melatonin products are frequently mislabeled and sometimes contaminated: actual content ranged from -83% to +478% of label, lot-to-lot variability up to 465%, and serotonin was detected in 8 of ~30 products. — <https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/full/10.5664/jcsm.6462>
- **[high]** Pediatric melatonin ingestions rose ~530% over 2012-2021 (260,435 reports; melatonin became the most-ingested substance reported for children in 2020), with rising hospitalizations, 5 children needing mechanical ventilation, and 2 deaths. — <https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/71/wr/mm7122a1.htm>
- **[low]** Chronic high-dose melatonin and adolescent HPG/puberty disruption is biologically plausible (kisspeptin-GnRH interaction; normal pubertal decline in nocturnal melatonin) but human evidence does not establish harm; long-term safety data are limited and low-certainty, with no adequately powered RCT. — <https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.2147/NSS.S181365>

**Conflicts / uncertainty:** Dose: low and high doses are equivalent for phase-shifting (Burgess 2010), yet Ferracioli-Oda found higher doses/longer trials gave somewhat larger sleep-onset/total-sleep effects — reconciled by a modest direct soporific effect that adds no chronobiotic benefit and raises grogginess/mistiming risk. Shift work disorder is the most contested of the four indications (older, small studies; hard-to-time doses) — MEDIUM-to-LOW. On pediatric safety, the Council for Responsible Nutrition (industry) reframed the CDC/JAMA rise as merely reflecting product popularity, conflicting with the clinical reading of rising hospitalizations and deaths — weight CDC/JAMA primary data over the trade rebuttal. HPG/puberty: mechanistic worry vs reassuring-but-underpowered long-term human data — assert neither harm nor confident safety.

**Do not absorb yet:**
- Claim that low-dose melatonin reliably resets the clock overnight or works like a switch — shifts are modest (<1 h/day), need correct DLMO-relative timing over days, and mistiming can shift the clock the wrong way
- Claim that more melatonin = better sleep — the hypnotic effect is small and plateaus; 5-10 mg OTC doses are supraphysiologic and not superior for core indications
- Claim that melatonin is proven safe long-term for children/adolescents, especially for puberty/HPG outcomes — currently low-confidence on both harm and safety
- Claim that AASM strongly recommends melatonin for DSPD — recommendations are weak/conditional on low-quality evidence
- Any claim that melatonin helps irregular sleep-wake rhythm in dementia — the guideline recommends against it
- Treating creator 'circadian biohacking' mg/timing protocols as validated — PRC timing is individualized to DLMO, rarely measured outside research

## Deep Research audit — Light dosing for circadian phase shifting vs popular claims (DR-008)

> Autonomous web deep-research (agent), cited to primary sources. Full report: [[DR-008]]. Note: [[DR-008-audit]]

**Executive summary:** Light resets the SCN via melanopsin ipRGCs (peak ~480 nm). Direction is set by timing relative to core body temperature minimum (CBTmin, ~2-3 h before wake): light after CBTmin (morning) advances phase, before it (evening) delays phase, with an insensitive midday zone. The intensity dose-response is logistic, not linear: Zeitzer 2000 found half-maximal illuminance ~120 lux for both melatonin phase resetting and suppression, with phase-shift saturation ~550 lux and suppression saturation ~200 lux; ~100 lux dim room light already gives ~half the max phase delay and ~88% of max melatonin suppression. So everyday indoor evening light is a meaningful delaying signal, and you don't need 10,000 lux to shift phase. The real basis for 'get morning sunlight' is that outdoor light (cloudy ~1,000-10,000 lux; sun ~50,000-100,000+ lux) far exceeds indoor (~100-500 lux), reliably saturating the morning advancing signal. Intermittent pulses are nearly as effective as continuous light, supporting short doses. Glass and sunglasses reduce dose; blue light is most potent. Where popular framing outruns evidence: precise 5/10/20-30 min cloud-cover numbers are heuristics not trial-validated, and downstream claims (cortisol/metabolism/immunity benefits from the brief morning dose) are mechanism-flavored extrapolations. Clinical light therapy (2,500-10,000 lux, 30 min-2 h, PRC-timed) is a separate, well-characterized therapeutic regime with only conditional/low-quality AASM recommendation strength.

**Key findings:**
- **[high]** Shift direction is set by timing relative to CBTmin: light after CBTmin (morning, ~2-3 h before to after wake) advances phase; before CBTmin (evening/early night) delays; midday is relatively insensitive. — <https://www.circadiansleepdisorders.org/info/PRC.php>
- **[high]** Intensity dose-response is logistic with half-maximal illuminance ~120 lux; phase-shift saturates ~550 lux, melatonin suppression ~200 lux; dim ~100 lux room light gives ~half max phase delay and ~88% of max melatonin suppression vs ~9100 lux (Zeitzer 2000, 6.5 h, early night). — <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10922269/>
- **[high]** Short-wavelength ~480 nm (blue) light is most potent for phase resetting and melatonin suppression via melanopsin; 460 nm > 555 nm at equal photon density. — <https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2205301119>
- **[high]** Indoor (~100-500 lux) and outdoor (overcast ~1,000-2,000+ lux; direct sun ~50,000-100,000+ lux) intensities differ by 1-3 orders of magnitude, which is the real justification for getting outdoor morning light. — <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8929548/>
- **[high]** Intermittent bright-light pulses (e.g., six 15-min pulses, ~23% of continuous duration) produce nearly the full resetting of 5 h continuous bright light, giving mechanistic support for short morning doses. — <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15039146/>
- **[high]** Consensus indoor recommendations: >=250 lux melanopic EDI daytime, <=10 lux evening (3+ h before bed), <=1 lux during sleep, measured at the eye. — <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8929548/>
- **[medium]** Light through closed eyelids can suppress melatonin (~25-56%) and shift DLMO (~17-71 min after 60-min exposures), but is strongly attenuated; evidence is preliminary/small-sample. — <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3469368/>
- **[medium]** Clinical bright light therapy for circadian sleep disorders uses 2,500-10,000 lux for 30 min-2 h timed to the PRC; morning light advances phase in DSWPD but AASM rates it conditional/low-quality evidence. — <https://stanfordhealthcare.org/medical-conditions/sleep/advanced-sleep-phase-syndrome/treatments/bright-light-therapy.html>
- **[low]** Popular '10 minutes of morning sunlight' guidance (outdoors, no glass, no sunglasses) is mechanistically reasonable for entrainment, but the exact minute thresholds and specific cortisol/metabolism/immunity outcome claims are extrapolations, not validated dosing trials. — <https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/using-light-for-health>

**Conflicts / uncertainty:** High-lux clinical protocols ('need bright light') vs demonstrated sensitivity at ~100 lux are reconcilable once you separate 'guaranteed strong/fast shift' (high lux) from 'threshold where light begins to matter' (tens of lux); the latter makes ordinary evening indoor light a real delaying signal. Through-window claims are overstated in popular framing: glass attenuates and removes UV but transmits much visible/blue light; the larger loss is indoor distance from windows, and glass-specific circadian attenuation is not well quantified in primary sources. Sex/chronotype/iris-color differences exist (women more sensitive to brightest light only; iris color null in one delay study) but are modest and do not overturn the general PRC. Attributing the morning cortisol awakening response to a brief light dose is weakly supported since the CAR is largely endogenous.

**Do not absorb yet:**
- Precise 5/10/20-30 minute cloud-cover dosing as validated thresholds (heuristics, not trial-derived)
- Downstream outcome claims (metabolism, immunity, focus, daytime cortisol spike) attributed to the brief morning light dose
- Strong quantitative claims about window-glass attenuation of circadian-effective light (under-specified in primary sources)
- Eyelid/light-mask phase shifting as a robust everyday tool (preliminary, small samples)
- That evening blue-blocking fully neutralizes screen effects (intensity and timing dominate; spectrum is a modifier)
