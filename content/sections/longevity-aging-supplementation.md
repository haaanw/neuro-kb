# Longevity, Aging & Supplementation

> Canonical synthesis from 23 ingested episodes (multi-creator). Confidence-graded; creator claims flagged where they outrun evidence.

## Overview

This section spans the spectrum from well-established geroscience to frontier and frankly speculative anti-aging marketing. Sources are science communicators (Huberman Lab, Peter Attia / The Drive, Rhonda Patrick / FoundMyFitness) interviewing researchers (David Sinclair, Tony Wyss-Coray, Steve Horvath, Martin Picard, Bill Harris, Roger Seheult) plus a peptide clinician. The signal-to-noise ratio varies enormously between episodes: the same library contains rigorous discussion of epigenetic aging clocks and omega-3 RCTs alongside personal NMN/peptide anecdotes presented with clinical-sounding confidence. The grading below is the load-bearing part of this page.

The central scientific story is that **aging is increasingly measurable**. Epigenetic clocks (Horvath, PhenoAge, GrimAge) derived from DNA methylation patterns predict mortality and healthspan better than chronological age, and this has matured into a legitimate research field. From this, several frameworks have grown: Sinclair's "information theory of aging" (aging as loss of epigenetic information / a noisy epigenome), the "hallmarks of aging" consensus (~8-9 causes), and nutrient-sensing pathways (sirtuins/NAD+, mTOR, AMPK, IGF-1) that link diet and fasting to cellular maintenance. The mechanisms are real; the leap from "this pathway exists in mice" to "this supplement extends human lifespan" is where most of the contested claims live.

On the **intervention** side, the evidence is a steep gradient. Strongest: caloric restriction / time-restricted eating slows aging markers in animals and improves metabolic health in humans; omega-3 (EPA/DHA, measured via Omega-3 Index) has robust cardiovascular and emerging biological-aging data; vitamin D corrects a genuinely common deficiency; exercise and muscle mass are among the best-validated longevity levers; protein adequacy protects against sarcopenia. Middling: creatine for cognition, metformin for longevity (the TAME trial rationale, but unproven in non-diabetics and possibly blunts exercise adaptations). Weakest / speculative: NMN and resveratrol for human lifespan, "young blood" / plasma factor rejuvenation, and most peptides for anti-aging.

A recurring tension is that the most prominent creators here have **commercial and reputational stakes** in supplement narratives (Sinclair especially with NMN/resveratrol; the peptide episode is essentially clinician opinion). Several present personal n=1 experiences ("I measured dozens of people," "I take a gram of NMN") as if they were trial data. Treat dosage and efficacy claims as hypotheses to verify against primary literature, not settled fact.

The practical takeaway across nearly all credible voices converges on unglamorous fundamentals: don't be metabolically sick (manage glucose/insulin, ApoB, blood pressure), build and keep muscle, get omega-3 and vitamin D to sufficiency, eat adequate protein, sleep, and don't smoke. The exotic supplements are, at best, the last 1-5%.

## Key findings by theme

### Epigenetic aging and biological clocks
- DNA-methylation clocks (Horvath multi-tissue clock, DNAm PhenoAge, GrimAge) measure biological age and predict mortality/healthspan better than chronological age. **[high]**
- Biological age can diverge from chronological age by 10-20 years between individuals; the clock advances fastest in early life (infancy/puberty) then roughly linearly. **[medium-high]**
- These clocks are validated as *predictors/biomarkers*; whether reversing a clock reading equals true rejuvenation of function is not established. **[medium]**
- Sinclair's "information theory of aging" — aging as accumulated epigenetic noise / loss of which-genes-are-on information, partly resettable — is a coherent, influential hypothesis, not consensus fact. The mouse work (inducing/accelerating aging via DNA breaks) is real; the "80% epigenetic, 20% genetic" figure is a heuristic, not a hard measured constant. **[low-medium]**

### Nutrient sensing: fasting, sirtuins, mTOR, NAD+
- Caloric restriction extends lifespan up to ~30-40% in rodents — among the most reproduced findings in biology. **[high]**
- In primates and dogs the picture is weaker and the magnitude does not transfer: the two landmark rhesus monkey studies disagreed on lifespan (Wisconsin showed a survival benefit; the NIA/NIH study found no lifespan extension in non-obese monkeys), so CR's primate benefits are clearer for healthspan than for lifespan. **[medium]**
- Time-restricted eating / skipping a meal lowers fasting glucose and insulin and improves metabolic markers in humans; lifespan extension in humans is inferred, not proven. **[medium-high]**
- Fasting upregulates autophagy; deeper "chaperone-mediated autophagy" engaging around days 2-3 is shown in animals. Human autophagy timing claims are extrapolated from mouse/cell data. **[medium]**
- Low insulin/IGF-1 activates sirtuins; low leucine/amino acids downregulates mTOR; the "sirtuin up, mTOR down" fasted state drives repair pathways. Pathway biology is solid; the framing that leucine/protein is "pro-aging" is an oversimplification that conflicts with the strong pro-muscle / anti-sarcopenia evidence (see Contested). **[medium]**
- NAD+ declines with age and is required for sirtuin activity. NMN/NR raise blood NAD+ in humans. That higher NAD+ extends human healthspan/lifespan is **not** demonstrated in RCTs. **[low for lifespan; medium for raising NAD+]**

### Omega-3 fatty acids
- Omega-3 Index (% EPA+DHA in red blood cell membranes) is a stable long-term marker (RBCs turn over ~120 days); target ~8%+, with most Western populations deficient (~4%). **[high]**
- Higher omega-3 status associates with lower cardiovascular and all-cause mortality; EPA/DHA reduce triglycerides and inflammation (SPMs / specialized pro-resolving mediators). **[high]**
- New evidence (e.g., DO-HEALTH analyses) suggests omega-3, especially combined with vitamin D + exercise, slows biological-aging clock measures. Effect sizes are modest and need replication. **[medium]**
- EPA specifically (above ~1.5 g/day, ideally 2-3 g/day) is linked to cognitive and mood benefit. **[medium-high]**

### Micronutrients: vitamin D, magnesium, and "triage"
- Vitamin D deficiency is widespread and racially skewed (NHANES: African-Americans far more likely deficient); correcting deficiency to sufficiency is justified, with immune and possibly respiratory-infection benefit. High-dose protocols (e.g., 60,000 IU/day x7 in hospitalized COVID) are clinical-trial specifics, not general guidance. **[high for correcting deficiency; medium for infection effects]**
- Rhonda Patrick's "triage theory" (Bruce Ames): under micronutrient shortfall the body prioritizes short-term survival over long-term repair, accelerating aging — a plausible mechanistic framing, not directly proven to extend lifespan. **[medium]**
- Sulforaphane (broccoli sprouts; +mustard seed powder to restore myrosinase in cooked broccoli) activates Nrf2/glutathione antioxidant pathways. Biochemistry is real; longevity benefit is extrapolated. **[medium]**

### Creatine for brain and body
- Creatine monohydrate ~5 g/day improves muscle performance and is among the best-evidenced sport supplements; cognitive benefit is clearest in those low on dietary creatine (vegetarians) or sleep-deprived. **[high for muscle; medium for cognition]**
- Emerging interest in higher brain-targeted doses (10 g/day, even 15-20 g) — Patrick calls 10 g a likely "new baseline." This is a forward-leaning opinion ahead of the trial evidence. **[low-medium]**

### Metformin, exercise, and the longevity-drug debate
- Metformin improves insulin sensitivity; the TAME trial rationale and diabetic-cohort epidemiology motivate testing it for longevity, but it is unproven in healthy/non-diabetic people. **[medium for rationale; low for healthy-person benefit]**
- Metformin may blunt exercise-induced mitochondrial/strength adaptations — a real tradeoff Attia flags; many in the field now favor exercise over metformin if forced to choose. **[medium]**
- Exercise (especially resistance training and maintaining muscle mass / VO2 max) is repeatedly cited as the single most robust longevity intervention available. **[high]**

### Mitochondria, "energetic stress," and rejuvenation frontier
- Mitochondrial function/biogenesis underlies energy and resilience; chronic stress and poor sleep impose an "energy debt" (Picard's framing). Mechanistically grounded; specific protocols are exploratory. **[medium]**
- "Young blood" / plasma-factor rejuvenation (Wyss-Coray): parabiosis and plasma factors rejuvenate aged tissue in mice; some factors identified. Human translation is early and unproven; commercial plasma-infusion clinics run far ahead of the data. **[low-medium for mechanism; low for human use]**
- Senescent ("zombie") cells accumulate with age, drive inflammation; excess iron increases senescence (Serrano lab). Senolytic strategies are promising in mice, early in humans. **[medium for biology; low for human senolytics]**

### Peptides
- Therapeutic peptides exist with legitimate uses; the ingested episode is largely a clinician's personal/anecdotal account (e.g., pinealon for REM, regeneration claims) and includes explicit liability/safety caveats around off-label dosing. Treat anti-aging peptide claims as **opinion**, not evidence. **[low]**

## Actionable protocols

Consolidated and deduped from the episodes. Dosages are *as stated by creators* — verify against current guidelines and your physician, especially for high-dose vitamin D, NMN, and peptides.

**Diet / fasting**
- Skip one meal per day (breakfast or dinner — keep the fast adjacent to sleep). Expect ~2-3 weeks of hunger/habit adjustment; ramp gradually rather than going "cold turkey." (Sinclair)
- Optional longer fasts (24-72 h) occasionally for deeper autophagy; harder and not necessary for baseline benefit. Use water + electrolytes if light-headed.
- A spoonful of yogurt / splash of milk / olive oil is unlikely to meaningfully "break" a metabolic fast — the body responds to glucose/insulin/mTOR, not a binary switch. (Sinclair)
- Protein target ~1 g per pound of body weight per day to preserve muscle (Attia, Wyss-Coray). Note this *competes* with strict mTOR-minimization framing — prioritize muscle in most adults.
- Finish eating ~3 h before bed.

**Supplements (creator-reported)**
- Omega-3: enough EPA/DHA to push Omega-3 Index to ~8%+; commonly 1-2 g, up to 2-3 g EPA/day for cognition. Re-test only after ~120 days (RBC turnover). (Harris, Patrick, Huberman)
- Vitamin D: correct deficiency to sufficiency (e.g., ~4,000 IU/day cited to restore levels); test, dose, re-test in ~120 days. Avoid copying acute hospital megadoses.
- Creatine monohydrate: 5 g/day baseline (muscle + cognition); some advocate up to 10 g/day for brain.
- Choline: ~500 mg-1 g/day for brain health. (Huberman)
- Sulforaphane: broccoli sprouts; add ~1 g ground mustard seed to cooked broccoli to restore sulforaphane yield (~4x). (Patrick)
- NMN (Sinclair's personal protocol, NOT a recommendation): ~1 g (sometimes 2 g)/day; ~2 weeks to roughly double blood NAD+. Efficacy for human longevity unproven.

**Lifestyle / tracking**
- Resistance training to build/maintain muscle mass; prioritize muscle and VO2 max as core longevity levers.
- Track blood work over years (not single snapshots): HbA1c/fasting glucose, hs-CRP (cardiovascular inflammation + mortality predictor), ApoB, ferritin/iron (excess iron may accelerate senescence — don't over-supplement).
- Sleep as the foundation; address it before exotic interventions.

## Contested / where creator framing outruns evidence

- **NMN / NR / resveratrol for human lifespan.** Raising NAD+ in blood is demonstrated; lifespan/healthspan extension in humans is not. Sinclair presents n=1 and "dozens of people I measured" as near-proof and has commercial ties; treat as unproven. **[low]**
- **"80% of aging is epigenetic, 20% genetic."** A memorable heuristic stated as fact; the real partition is not a measured universal constant. **[low]**
- **Leucine/protein as broadly "pro-aging."** True that leucine activates mTOR, but minimizing protein conflicts with strong evidence that adequate protein and muscle mass protect healthspan, especially in older adults. The mTOR-bad framing is context-dependent, not a directive to eat less protein. **[contested]**
- **Reversing an epigenetic clock = rejuvenation.** Clock reversal is a biomarker change; functional rejuvenation in humans is not established. **[low]**
- **"Young blood" / plasma infusions** for human anti-aging: mouse parabiosis is real science; human commercial use is unsupported. **[low]**
- **Metformin for longevity in healthy people.** Unproven and may blunt exercise gains. **[low]**
- **Anti-aging peptides** (pinealon, regenerative peptides): clinician anecdote with self-acknowledged safety/liability caveats; not evidence-grade. **[low]**
- **High-dose creatine (10-20 g/day) as "new baseline" for brain.** Promising direction, ahead of the trial data. **[low-medium]**
